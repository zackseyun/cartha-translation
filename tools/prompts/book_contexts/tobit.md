# Tobit — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX — two major recensions: **Sinaiticus (S)** (longer, generally preferred as more original) and **Vaticanus/Alexandrinus (BA)** (shorter, more polished). Qumran Aramaic and Hebrew fragments (4Q196–200) support the Sinaiticus-type text. Our base is Sinaiticus.
- **Form**: Novelistic narrative / diaspora tale. 14 chapters. A self-contained story combining wisdom themes, angelic mediation, and a romance subplot.
- **Date**: ca. 3rd–2nd c. BC. Setting: Nineveh diaspora, but the story's concerns are post-exilic Jewish identity.
- **Original audience**: Diaspora Jews. The book instructs on: burial of the dead, almsgiving, prayer, maintaining Jewish identity (endogamy), and trusting divine providence.

## Key translation issues

1. **The two recensions**: Sinaiticus (our base) is longer and more Semitic in character. When the text is uncertain, cite which recension a given reading follows.
2. **ἐλεημοσύνη** ("almsgiving / charitable giving"): Tobit is obsessed with almsgiving as a meritorious act. Always render as "almsgiving" — not "acts of mercy" or "kindness." The specific practice of giving money/food to the poor is what is meant.
3. **Raphael / "Azariah"**: the angelic disguise. Keep "Azariah" in the narrative sections before the revelation (6:6–12:15) and "Raphael" in the narrator's voice and after 12:15. The tension of the disguise is part of the story.
4. **κύριε** ("Lord / master"): in some passages, addressing Raphael as "Lord/master" is socially conventional (as a hired guide); in others it approaches divine address. Context determines rendering — do not flatten.
5. **The Prayer of Tobit** (ch. 3) and **the Prayer of Sarah** (ch. 3): both are formal liturgical prayers. Preserve the elevated prayer register.
6. **The wedding night ritual** (ch. 8): Tobias's prayer over the fish organs. Keep the ritual elements intact — the fumigation and the prayer are specific cultural acts.
7. **The Song of Tobit** (ch. 13): hymn of praise and eschatological vision of Jerusalem rebuilt. Elevated lyric register.

## Style / register

- Narrative prose with embedded prayers and hymns.
- The story has a warm, almost folk-tale quality; do not over-formalize the narrative sections.
- The prayers and hymns (chs. 3, 8, 13) lift into elevated register; keep the contrast with the narrative.
- Dialogue has natural conversational quality (especially Tobit/Tobias, Anna/Tobit exchanges).

## Known pitfalls

- **Mixing Sinaiticus and Vaticanus readings without flagging.** Note which recension is being translated.
- **Rendering ἐλεημοσύνη as generic "mercy."** This book has a specific theology of almsgiving as meritorious act.
- **Losing the narrative warmth.** Tobit is a well-crafted story; the translation should read as a good story.
