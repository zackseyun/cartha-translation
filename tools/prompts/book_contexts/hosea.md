# Hosea — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 14 chapters.
- **Form**: Prophetic oracles — mostly poetry. Biographical section (1–3) involving Hosea's marriage as a sign-act, then oracles of judgment and restoration (4–14).
- **Date**: ca. 760–720 BC (before the fall of Samaria, 722 BC). Northern kingdom prophet.
- **Original audience**: The northern kingdom of Israel under the metaphor of an unfaithful wife.

## Key translation issues

1. **The marriage metaphor**: Hosea's marriage to Gomer and Israel's "whoring after" other gods are inextricably linked. The sexual/faithfulness language is the book's central metaphor. Do not sanitize — "whoredom" (זְנוּנִים) should be rendered with its sexual directness.
2. **חֶסֶד** (2:19; 6:6 — "steadfast love / loyal love"): "I desire steadfast love (חֶסֶד) and not sacrifice" (6:6). In Hosea, חֶסֶד describes the covenant loyalty Israel has abandoned. Keep "steadfast love" — not "mercy" (which loses the covenant dimension).
3. **שׁוּב** ("to return / repent"): The call to repentance in 14:1 — "Return, O Israel, to the LORD your God." Consistent with the Jeremiah/OT usage — keep "return."
4. **"Ephraim"** as a name for the northern kingdom: Hosea often uses "Ephraim" as a synonym for Israel/northern kingdom. Keep "Ephraim" — do not substitute "Israel" unless the Hebrew reads Israel.
5. **The wilderness/betrothal oracle** (2:14–23): "I will allure her and bring her into the wilderness, and speak tenderly to her." The voice shifts from judgment to tender restoration. The contrast must be audible.
6. **"Out of Egypt I called my son"** (11:1): A text cited in Matthew 2:15. Render the Hebrew as it stands — a historical statement about the exodus. Matthew's re-reading belongs in a NT footnote, not in the Hosea text.
7. **The text-critical difficulties**: Hosea's Hebrew is frequently corrupt or difficult. Where the MT is unclear, footnote the reading; do not silently smooth.

## Style / register

- Emotional, passionate, alternating between divine anguish and tender appeal.
- The poetry is dense and sometimes obscure. Preserve the density rather than over-clarifying.
- Chapter 11 ("When Israel was a child…") is one of the most emotionally powerful passages in the OT — a parent's love for an unfaithful child.

## Known pitfalls

- **Sanitizing the harlotry/whoredom language.** The directness is the prophetic point.
- **Smoothing the MT textual difficulties** — flag them rather than silently choosing.
- **Pre-interpreting 11:1 through Matthew** — translate the OT text on its own terms.
