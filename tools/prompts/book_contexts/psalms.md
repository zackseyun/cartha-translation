# Psalms — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) and DSS Psalms scrolls (11QPsalms^a) for text-critical reference. 150 chapters (Hebrew numbering; LXX adds Psalm 151).
- **Form**: Lyric poetry anthology in five "books" (1–41, 42–72, 73–89, 90–106, 107–150). Genres: hymns of praise, laments (individual and communal), thanksgiving, royal psalms, wisdom psalms, pilgrimage songs (Psalms of Ascent, 120–134), penitential psalms.
- **Date**: Spans roughly 1000–400 BC; the collection is diverse.
- **Original audience**: Israel's worship — temple liturgy, pilgrimage, personal prayer.

## Key translation issues — general

1. **Parallelism**: the structural principle of Hebrew poetry. Semantic, synthetic, and antithetic parallelism. The second line of a couplet deepens, intensifies, specifies, or contrasts with the first — it does not merely repeat it. Render so both lines do distinct work.
2. **The divine name יהוה**: always "the LORD" (small caps). Keep the frequency of the name audible — many psalms pivot on its repetition.
3. **נֶפֶשׁ** ("soul / self / being"): In psalms it often means "I / my whole being / my life-breath." Context determines whether "soul," "I," or "my life" is best. Do not default to "soul" when "I" would carry the sense more accurately.
4. **חֶסֶד** ("steadfast love / loyal love / lovingkindness"): the most theologically weighted word in the Psalter (appears ~120 times in Psalms). "Steadfast love" (NRSV) or "loyal love" captures both the covenant-relational and emotional dimensions better than "mercy" or "kindness." Be consistent within a given psalm.
5. **צְדָקָה / מִשְׁפָּט** ("righteousness / justice"): often paired. "Righteousness" for צְדָקָה (right relationship, conformity to covenant standard), "justice" for מִשְׁפָּט (judgment, legal vindication). Do not reverse.
6. **Selah** (סֶלָה): keep "Selah" — meaning is uncertain but the term functions as a liturgical marker. Do not replace with "Pause" or omit.
7. **Superscriptions**: the psalm titles (e.g., "A Psalm of David," "For the director of music, with stringed instruments") are part of the MT text. Render them; do not silently drop them.

## Genre-specific guidance

- **Laments** (e.g., Pss. 3, 13, 22, 88): preserve the raw emotional honesty. The complaint forms ("How long, O LORD?" / "Why have you forgotten me?") are real questions, not rhetorical. Do not soften.
- **Praise / Hallelujah Psalms** (113–118, 146–150): keep the imperative force of the calls to praise.
- **Royal Psalms** (2, 45, 72, 110): preserve the royal-theological language without prematurely Christianizing it. Footnote NT applications but render the Hebrew sense first.
- **Psalm 22**: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1) — keep exactly; it is cited in Jesus's passion.
- **Imprecatory Psalms** (58, 109, 137): render the curses accurately, including the most difficult ones (137:9). These are the text; do not soften.

## Style / register

- Every psalm is poetry; render as verse where possible.
- Elevated, often archaic or liturgical register is appropriate.
- Preserve rhetorical questions, imperatives, repeated refrains, and acrostic structures (Pss. 9/10, 25, 34, 37, 111, 112, 119, 145) as closely as possible.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening parallelism** — treating the second line as a mere repetition of the first.
- **Over-rendering נֶפֶשׁ as "soul"** where "I" or "my life" is what the Hebrew means.
- **Softening lament and imprecation.** The psalmists said what they said.
