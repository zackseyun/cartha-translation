# Nahum — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for text-critical reference.
- **Form**: Prophetic poetry, almost entirely oracle. Three chapters. Opens with an acrostic poem (1:2–8, partial aleph-beth). Contains some of the most vivid battle poetry in the Hebrew Bible.
- **Date**: Pre-exilic. Nineveh fell in 612 BC; the book seems to anticipate this fall (3:1–19 reads as announcement/taunt). Composition ca. 630–612 BC.
- **Original audience**: Judah, under Assyrian oppression. The entire book is a celebration and announcement of Nineveh's destruction.

## Key translation issues

1. **The theophanic opening** (1:2–8): Yahweh as a divine warrior — jealous, avenging, slow to anger but great in power. The Hebrew is dense and highly charged. Keep the martial, cosmic imagery — do not soften.
2. **קִנֵּא / נוֹקֵם** (1:2 — "jealous / avenging"): God's jealousy here is covenantal protectiveness, not petty envy. "Vengeful" or "avenging" for נוֹקֵם — not "punishing" (too clinical).
3. **הֵיכָלָהּ / נִינְוֵה** throughout: Nineveh is personified as a woman/queen who will be stripped and shamed (3:4–7). The gendered imagery is deliberate and explicit in the Hebrew — render honestly including the undressing imagery.
4. **Battle sequences** (2:3–13; 3:1–3): extremely rapid, staccato Hebrew lines — "chariots race… spears gleam… dead bodies are numberless." Preserve the breathless, staccato rhythm as much as possible in English.
5. **Taunt-song conventions** (3:8–17): comparison with Thebes (No-Amon), the rhetorical questions, the satire of Nineveh's defenses. Keep the taunting register.

## Style / register

- Intensely vivid prophetic poetry. The register is martial, triumphant, and at times grotesque.
- Do not moralize or distance the text from its raw celebration of an enemy's downfall. This is exactly what the author wrote.
- The acrostic opening is a formal device; render as best English allows while staying close to the sense.

## Known pitfalls

- **Softening the gendered shaming imagery at 3:4–7.** The Hebrew is explicit; the translation should not be more modest than the author was.
- **Losing the speed of the battle descriptions.** Short Hebrew clauses should map to short English clauses where possible.
- **Translating "No-Amon" (3:8) without noting it is Thebes.** Keep "No-Amon" (the Hebrew form) and footnote the identification.
