# Baruch — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Ziegler). Five chapters. The Letter of Jeremiah (ch. 6) is treated as a separate book in this corpus; this file covers Baruch 1–5.
- **Form**: Composite: prose introduction (1:1–14), penitential prayer (1:15–3:8), wisdom poem (3:9–4:4), consolation poems (4:5–5:9).
- **Date**: Composite composition, likely 2nd–1st c. BC. Attribution to Baruch son of Neriah (Jeremiah's scribe) is literary convention.
- **Original audience**: Post-exilic Jewish community; the prayer envisions the exile context (Babylonian) as a lens for the current community's situation.

## Key translation issues

1. **The Confession** (1:15–3:8): structured as a deuteronomic confession of sin and plea for restoration, echoing Daniel 9 extensively. Render the liturgical-confessional register faithfully.
2. **The Wisdom Poem** (3:9–4:4): identifies Wisdom with Torah ("She is the book of the commandments of God," 4:1). The key statement "this is our God" (3:36–37) requires careful rendering — it is a significant theological claim about Wisdom/Torah's divine character.
3. **συνέσει** / "understanding" vs "wisdom": in 3:9–38, σοφία and σύνεσις both appear as near-synonyms for divine wisdom; keep them distinct but parallel.
4. **The Consolation** (4:5–5:9): echoes Deutero-Isaiah (Isaiah 40–66). The personification of Jerusalem as a grieving mother who will see her children return is important — keep the maternal personification.
5. **Baruch 5:1–9** parallels 1 Enoch 1:4 and Psalms of Solomon 11. The eschatological vision of God leading Israel home should be rendered with the elevated register of the Isaian source.

## Style / register

- Mixed: the confession is corporate liturgical prose; the wisdom poem is elevated didactic poetry; the consolation poems are lyric.
- The tonal shifts between sections are significant; preserve them.
- Do not flatten the liturgical register of the prayer sections into casual prose.
