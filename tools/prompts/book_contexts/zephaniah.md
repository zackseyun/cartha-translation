# Zephaniah — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for text-critical reference.
- **Form**: Prophetic poetry with some prose. Three chapters. Superscription connects Zephaniah to Hezekiah's lineage and Josiah's reign.
- **Date**: Pre-exilic, early Josianic period (ca. 640–609 BC). The social critique of Jerusalem's syncretism (1:4–6; 3:1–4) fits the pre-Josianic reform context.
- **Original audience**: Judah in a period of religious syncretism and social corruption, before Josiah's reform.

## Key translation issues

1. **יוֹם יהוה** ("the Day of the LORD"): Zephaniah's central concept. 1:14–18 is the most intense "Day of the LORD" passage in the prophetic corpus ("a day of wrath… a day of distress… a day of darkness and gloom…"). Render consistently with the fixed phrase "the Day of the LORD." The accumulation of "day of X" phrases in 1:15 is rhetorical climax — keep every parallel.
2. **הַסֵּף / כֶּסֶף / זָהָב** (1:18): "Their silver and their gold will not be able to deliver them." The metallic vocabulary in the judgment sections is precise — "silver" and "gold" are not generic "wealth."
3. **עֲנָוִים / עֲנָוֵי הָאָרֶץ** (2:3 — "the humble / the meek of the land"): key term in the "remnant" theology. "The humble," "the poor in spirit," and "the meek" all map to this. "The humble of the land" is literal and accurate.
4. **שְׁאֵרִית** ("remnant"): the concept of the surviving faithful remnant (2:7, 9; 3:13). Keep "remnant" as the stable translation.
5. **The joy song** (3:14–20): a closing reversal from judgment to celebration. The imperatives ("Sing! Shout! Rejoice!") should be rendered with the energy the Hebrew carries.

## Style / register

- Urgent prophetic poetry. The judgment oracles have intense emotional charge.
- The "Day of the LORD" section (1:14–18) should feel climactic and overwhelming — it was the source for the medieval *Dies Irae* hymn.
- The closing reversal (3:14–20) is joyful and elevated; the contrast with the preceding judgment must be felt.

## Known pitfalls

- **Softening the judgment imagery.** The text does not soften it; the translation should not either.
- **Losing the formulaic parallel structure of 1:15.** The accumulation of "day of X, day of Y…" is a rhetorical device; render each member of the list.
