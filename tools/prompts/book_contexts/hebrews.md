# Hebrews — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 13 chapters.
- **Form**: Theological homily with exhortation sections woven throughout. Not a conventional letter. The most literary Greek in the NT.
- **Date**: ca. 60–90 AD. Before or after 70 AD is debated (whether the Temple sacrifices are still ongoing is contested).
- **Original audience**: Jewish Christians being tempted to revert to Judaism; the book argues for the superiority of Christ over the Levitical sacrificial system.

## Key translation issues

1. **The Levitical typology vocabulary**: the entire letter requires consistent rendering of cultic terms — ἱερεύς (priest), ἀρχιερεύς (high priest), θυσία (sacrifice), προσφορά (offering), σκηνή (tabernacle/tent), ἅγια ἁγίων (holy of holies), κατεπέτασμα (veil/curtain), ἐφάπαξ (once for all). These must be stable throughout.
2. **κρείττων / κρείσσων** ("better"): the key structural comparative — "Jesus is better than angels" (1:4), "a better covenant" (7:22; 8:6), "better promises" (8:6), "better sacrifice" (9:23), "better homeland" (11:16). Always render "better" — this is the homily's structural spine.
3. **πίστις** ("faith"): chapter 11 defines faith as "ὑπόστασις πραγμάτων ἐλπιζομένων, ἔλεγχος οὐ βλεπομένων" (11:1). "The substance / assurance of things hoped for, the conviction of things not seen." Render accurately: "substance" (or "assurance") and "conviction" (not "evidence" for ἔλεγχος, which carries a more formal logical connotation than the English "evidence" usually does).
4. **ἀρχηγός** (2:10; 12:2 — "pioneer / author / founder"): "Pioneer" is defensible for the one who goes first; "author" overreads the literary sense. "Pioneer and perfecter of our faith" (12:2) — "perfecter" (τελειωτής, a NT hapax) should be rendered as "one who brings to completion."
5. **The "rest" of Psalm 95 / Hebrews 3–4**: the sustained midrash on Psalm 95:7–11 uses κατάπαυσις (rest/cessation). Render "rest" consistently throughout the extended argument.
6. **ἐφάπαξ** (7:27; 9:12; 10:10 — "once for all"): technically distinct from ἅπαξ ("once"). Keep "once for all" — the emphasis is on the non-repeatable finality of Christ's sacrifice.
7. **The "cloud of witnesses" (νέφος μαρτύρων, 12:1)**: "cloud" — not "crowd." The imagery of athletic spectators looking on from a stadium gallery is the point.

## Style / register

- The most sophisticated Greek in the NT. The rhetoric is deliberate and sustained.
- Preserve the elaborate sentence structures — in particular the conditional warnings (2:1–4; 3:7–4:13; 5:11–6:12; 10:26–39; 12:25–29).
- The exhortation sections ("let us…") carry urgent pastoral weight; keep the imperative force.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying "better" for English style.** The structural spine of the argument depends on the repeated comparative.
- **Under-rendering ἐφάπαξ as merely "once."** The theological freight of "once for all" is the point.
