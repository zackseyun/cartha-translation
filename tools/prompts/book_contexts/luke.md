# Luke — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 24 chapters.
- **Form**: Gospel narrative. The most literary Greek of the four Gospels. Written as a two-volume work with Acts. Opens with a formal literary prologue (1:1–4) in classical Greek style.
- **Date**: ca. 80–90 AD.
- **Original audience**: Theophilus (named patron) and Gentile Christian readers; Luke emphasizes Jesus's concern for the poor, women, Samaritans, and Gentiles.

## Key translation issues

1. **The literary prologue** (1:1–4): Written in elevated classical Greek ("Inasmuch as many have undertaken to compile a narrative…"). Render with appropriate literary register — not colloquial.
2. **Infancy narratives** (1–2): Luke's birth and childhood stories use elevated, Semitic-flavored prose (the Magnificat, Benedictus, Nunc Dimittis — 1:46–55, 1:68–79, 2:29–32). These are hymns: render them as poetry with their full liturgical weight.
3. **ἀγαπητός / χαρίτωσεν** ("beloved / highly favored"): "Hail, highly favored one" (1:28) — not "full of grace" (which imports a later theological gloss). "Highly favored" or "one who has found favor" is accurate.
4. **σωτήρ / σωτηρία** ("savior / salvation"): The Lucan vocabulary of salvation is prominent (1:47, 2:11, 19:9). Render consistently.
5. **The Sermon on the Plain** (6:17–49): Luke's version differs from Matthew's Sermon on the Mount. Do not harmonize — Luke has "Blessed are you who are poor" (second-person plural), Matthew has "Blessed are the poor in spirit" (third-person). Preserve Luke's own form.
6. **Parables unique to Luke**: Good Samaritan (10:25–37), Prodigal Son (15:11–32), Rich Man and Lazarus (16:19–31), Pharisee and Tax Collector (18:9–14). Each has a distinctive narrative style; render the characters' voices distinctly.
7. **εὐαγγελίζομαι / εὐαγγέλιον** ("to proclaim good news / the good news"): Luke uses the verb form heavily — "he went about proclaiming the good news." Keep the verb alive.

## Style / register

- The most polished Greek in the Gospels. Luke is a skilled Hellenistic historian.
- Infancy narratives shift to a more Semitic, LXX-influenced register — a deliberate stylistic choice; preserve it.
- Parables: simple, vivid, story-telling register. Render characters' speeches in their appropriate voice (the prodigal's father speaks warmly; the elder son speaks resentfully).
- Travel narrative (9:51–19:44): structurally framed by journey-to-Jerusalem; preserve the progression.

## Known pitfalls

- **Harmonizing Luke to Matthew** — especially in the Sermon on the Plain and the Lord's Prayer (11:2–4 vs. Matt 6:9–13). Luke's versions are shorter and different; keep them.
- **Flattening the literary prologue** into informal prose — it is deliberately elevated.
- **Missing the social reversals** (the Magnificat's "he has brought down the mighty… exalted the humble") — the verb tenses are aorists; render as completed action.
