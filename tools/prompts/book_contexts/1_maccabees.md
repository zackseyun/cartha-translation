# 1 Maccabees — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Hanhart). Composed in Hebrew; our text is the Greek translation. 16 chapters.
- **Form**: Historical narrative of the Maccabean revolt (167–134 BC), covering Mattathias, Judas, Jonathan, and Simon Maccabeus.
- **Date**: Composition ca. 100 BC. The author is pro-Hasmonean and writes from within that tradition.
- **Original audience**: Jewish readers in Judea, celebrating and legitimizing Hasmonean rule.

## Key translation issues

1. **Register**: 1 Maccabees deliberately imitates the style of the Historical Books (Joshua–Kings). Render with the same historical-chronicle register as OT narrative — not as Hellenistic historiography.
2. **νόμος / τόρα** ("the Law"): always "the Law" with the definite article in legal/religious context — not "the rules" or "the legislation."
3. **βδέλυγμα ἐρημώσεως** (1:54 — "the desolating abomination"): the altar set up by Antiochus. Render literally "the desolating abomination" — this is the technical phrase echoed in Daniel 9:27 and cited in the NT.
4. **ζῆλος** ("zeal"): Mattathias's defining virtue (2:27, 50, 58). Keep "zeal" — it is a theological term invoking Phinehas (Numbers 25). Do not render as "eagerness" or "enthusiasm."
5. **Speeches**: the book contains formal speeches (Mattathias's deathbed speech, ch. 2; speeches before battle). These are elevated formal prose; preserve the rhetoric.
6. **Documentary texts**: embedded official letters and decrees (chs. 8, 11–15). Render with official-administrative register.

## Style / register

- Historical-chronicle narrative; imitates the Deuteronomistic History in style.
- Battle accounts are straightforward narrative; do not dramatize beyond the text.
- Speeches and documents require elevated register.

## Known pitfalls

- **Rendering as Hellenistic historiography** (like 2 Maccabees). The two books have very different styles.
- **Under-translating ζῆλος** — this is a covenantal, zealous commitment, not casual enthusiasm.
