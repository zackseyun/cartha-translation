# Isaiah — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsa^a) for text-critical comparison. LXX (Ziegler critical edition). 66 chapters.
- **Form**: Prophetic poetry and prose. Conventionally divided: First Isaiah (1–39, 8th c. BC Jerusalem/Assyrian context), Deutero-Isaiah (40–55, exilic, Babylonian context), Trito-Isaiah (56–66, post-exilic). For translation purposes, render the unified MT text.
- **Date**: 8th–6th c. BC in composite. Final form post-exilic.
- **Original audience**: Judah across multiple historical crises (Assyrian threat, Babylonian exile, return). The book is both warning and consolation.

## Key translation issues

1. **קָדוֹשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל** ("the Holy One of Israel"): Isaiah's signature epithet for God (appears 25 times, mostly in Isaiah). Always render as "the Holy One of Israel" — not "the holy God of Israel" or variants.
2. **עֶבֶד יהוה** ("the Servant of the LORD"): the four Servant Songs (42:1–4; 49:1–6; 50:4–9; 52:13–53:12). Render as "the servant" — the identity is deliberately ambiguous in the text; do not preemptively identify as Israel or the Messiah in the translation itself. Footnote interpretive options.
3. **Isaiah 53**: "Suffering Servant" passage. Heavy NT usage (Matthew 8:17; Luke 22:37; Romans 10:16; etc.). Render from the Hebrew MT; note NT citations in footnotes, not in the translation. Key word: **נָשָׂא** ("to bear / to carry") — "bore our griefs," not "took away" (the latter adds a causative sense the Hebrew does not have).
4. **נַחֵם / נַחֲמוּ** (40:1 — "Comfort, comfort"): the double imperative that opens Deutero-Isaiah. Keep the double — "Comfort, comfort my people."
5. **עָלְמָה** (7:14): "young woman" (MT) vs. "virgin" (LXX παρθένος). The Hebrew word means "young woman of marriageable age" — not specifically "virgin" (בְּתוּלָה would be the precise Hebrew word). Render "young woman" with a footnote noting the LXX παρθένος reading and its NT significance (Matthew 1:23). Do not render "virgin" in the main text without a note.
6. **שָׁלוֹם**: render as "peace" (not "prosperity" or "welfare" alone — both are too narrow). Footnote the fuller semantic range when a verse turns on it.
7. **Chapters 24–27** ("Isaiah Apocalypse"): highly distinctive eschatological poetry; elevated register.

## Style / register

- Elevated prophetic poetry throughout. Isaiah is the most literary of the prophets.
- The oracles of judgment (chs. 1–12; 28–35) have sharp, confrontational rhetoric.
- Deutero-Isaiah (40–55) is characterized by lyric consolation and expansive rhetoric ("lift up your eyes on high"; "who has measured the waters in the hollow of his hand?").
- Trito-Isaiah (56–66) mixes consolation with social critique.
- The Servant Songs require sustained poetic attention.

## Known pitfalls

- **Rendering 7:14 with "virgin" without noting the Hebrew-Greek difference.**
- **Pre-Christianizing the Servant Songs.** Render the Hebrew; footnote the NT connection.
- **Losing the sheer literary power of Deutero-Isaiah.** These chapters are high Hebrew poetry; render accordingly.
