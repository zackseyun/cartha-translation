# John — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 21 chapters.
- **Form**: Gospel narrative. Distinct from the Synoptics in structure, vocabulary, and theology. Opens with prologue (1:1–18), then seven signs, the Farewell Discourse (13–17), passion narrative, resurrection appearances.
- **Date**: ca. 90–100 AD.
- **Original audience**: A community with deep theological reflection on the identity of Jesus, dealing with both Jewish exclusion and docetic tendencies.

## Key translation issues

1. **Λόγος** (1:1 — "the Word"): "In the beginning was the Word." Keep "Word" — it is the traditional rendering of the theological concept. Do not substitute "reason," "message," or "expression." The prologue (1:1–18) should be rendered as elevated theology-poetry.
2. **ἀλήθεια** ("truth"): in John, "truth" is not abstract — it is the reality revealed in Jesus (14:6: "I am the way, the truth, and the life"). The article is almost always present; keep it. "The truth" carries weight "truth" does not.
3. **The "I am" sayings** (ἐγώ εἰμι): absolute use at 8:58 ("before Abraham was, I am") and in the metaphorical predications ("I am the bread of life," "the light of the world," "the gate," "the good shepherd," "the resurrection and the life," "the way, the truth, and the life," "the vine"). For the absolute ἐγώ εἰμι (8:58; 13:19; 18:5–6), render with the same Exodus 3:14 resonance — "I am" not "I am he."
4. **ἀγάπη / φιλέω**: both words appear in the post-resurrection dialogue (21:15–17). The alternation may or may not be significant; do not force a sharp semantic distinction in translation, but footnote the question.
5. **μονογενής** (1:14, 18; 3:16, 18): "only-begotten" (traditional) or "only" or "unique." "Only" is increasingly preferred in scholarship; "only-begotten" has patristic baggage. Either is defensible; pick one and footnote the options.
6. **κόσμος** ("world"): John uses "world" both as the domain of God's love (3:16) and as the hostile realm opposed to Jesus (15:18–19). Context determines which register; do not collapse the distinction.
7. **The Farewell Discourse** (13–17): extended theological speech; the grammar of 14:1–31 is elaborate. Render the Greek syntax carefully.

## Style / register

- The Johannine style is distinctive: short declarative sentences, repetition, antithesis (light/darkness, above/below, truth/lies), dualistic vocabulary.
- Preserve the repetition — "I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live, and whoever lives and believes in me shall never die" — the structure is deliberate.
- The Prologue (1:1–18) is elevated theological poetry-prose; render it with that elevation.
- Dialogues (Nicodemus, Samaritan woman, "I am the bread of life" discourse) have a layered quality where speakers talk past each other; preserve the narrative irony.

## Known pitfalls

- **Harmonizing John to the Synoptics.** John's distinctive vocabulary and theology should be rendered on its own terms.
- **Importing Synoptic wording.** John's Jesus speaks differently; keep the Johannine voice.
- **Rendering ἐγώ εἰμι at 8:58 as "I have been."** This destroys the point of the grammar.
