# Joshua — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 24 chapters.
- **Form**: Narrative — the conquest and settlement of Canaan. Two parts: the conquest (1–12) and land allotment (13–21), with concluding covenant renewals (22–24).
- **Date**: Final deuteronomistic editing likely 7th–6th cent. BC.
- **Original audience**: Israel's settlement narrative — the fulfillment of the land promise.

## Key translation issues

1. **חֵרֶם** ("the ban / devoted to destruction / utterly destroy"): The מִלְחֶמֶת חֵרֶם — the commanded destruction of Canaanite populations. Render "the ban" or "devote to destruction" — not "utterly destroyed" alone (which loses the religious-legal concept of consecration to YHWH). Footnote the concept.
2. **חָזַק וְאַמֵּץ** (1:6, 7, 9, 18 — "be strong and courageous"): The repeated command to Joshua. Always "be strong and courageous" — keep the exact wording wherever it recurs.
3. **הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה** ("to this day"): The narrative formula explaining the ongoing significance of events. "And it is called X to this day." Render literally — not "even now" or "to the present."
4. **The Rahab narrative** (2; 6:22–25): חֶסֶד = "steadfast love / kindness" — Rahab requests הַחֶסֶד (kindness) in return for her help. Keep "kindness" or "steadfast love" to preserve the covenant-language echo.
5. **The land allotment sections** (13–21): Preserve all the geographical proper names accurately. These chapters are repetitive and formulaic — do not compress or summarize.
6. **Joshua's farewell** (23–24): The covenant renewal at Shechem (24) includes the famous "Choose this day whom you will serve… as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD." Render with the full rhetorical weight of a covenant choice.

## Style / register

- Narrative prose: military campaigns, miracle accounts (Jordan crossing, Jericho), and geographical catalogues.
- The geographical allotment sections are deliberately dry lists — do not enliven artificially.
- Joshua's speeches (1; 23–24) are hortatory Deuteronomistic rhetoric.

## Known pitfalls

- **Softening חֵרֶם** — render accurately and let footnotes address the ethical issues.
- **Varying "be strong and courageous"** across its multiple occurrences.
- **Compressing the land allotment lists** — they are intended to be comprehensive.
