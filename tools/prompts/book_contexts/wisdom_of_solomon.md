# Wisdom of Solomon — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Ziegler critical edition). Composed directly in Greek — no Hebrew original. 19 chapters.
- **Form**: Wisdom address in three sections: (1) chs. 1–6: exhortation to rulers, the fate of the righteous vs. ungodly; (2) chs. 6–9: praise of Wisdom (σοφία) as a divine attribute and Solomon's prayer; (3) chs. 10–19: historical survey (Exodus typology) — how Wisdom guided Israel and punished Egypt.
- **Date**: ca. 1st c. BC–1st c. AD. Alexandria, Egypt. Written in polished Hellenistic Greek.
- **Original audience**: Hellenized Jews in Egypt, perhaps under threat of apostasy or persecution. The book argues for the superiority of Jewish wisdom over Egyptian idolatry.

## Key translation issues

1. **σοφία** ("Wisdom"): hypostatic personification throughout. In chapters 7–9 Wisdom is described in terms that echo Greek philosophical concepts (φίλη / φίλος, "friend"; πνεῦμα, "spirit"; ἀτμίς, "vapor"). Keep "Wisdom" consistently; preserve the personification without either under- or over-reading it as hypostatic deity.
2. **ἀθανασία / ἀφθαρσία** ("immortality / incorruptibility"): these are key theological terms (2:23; 3:4; 8:13, 17; 15:3). Render precisely — "immortality" for ἀθανασία, "incorruptibility" for ἀφθαρσία — they are not synonyms.
3. **δικαιοσύνη** ("righteousness / justice"): used both ethically and eschatologically. Context determines which English register fits.
4. **ψυχή** ("soul"): used in a way that reflects both Jewish and Platonic influences. The "souls of the righteous are in the hand of God" (3:1) — "soul" is the right rendering here; do not substitute "people" or "they."
5. **The Egypt polemic** (chs. 11–19): extended comparison between Israel and Egypt. Preserves the detailed Exodus allusions. Do not flatten the specific biblical typology.
6. **The Idol Polemic** (chs. 13–15): philosophical argument against idol worship. Dense Hellenistic argumentation; render the logic clearly.

## Style / register

- Highly polished Hellenistic Greek prose and poetry. The register is elevated throughout — more literary than practical.
- The praise of Wisdom (7:22–8:1) is lyric poetry; render with appropriate elevation.
- The historical survey (chs. 10–19) is didactic-narrative.

## Known pitfalls

- **Importing Platonic dualism** (soul vs. body) further than the text warrants. The book uses Greek concepts but remains within a Jewish theological framework.
- **Under-distinguishing ἀθανασία and ἀφθαρσία.** Both matter theologically.
- **Flattening the idol polemic** — the three-stage argument (natural-theology error → idolatry → immorality) has its own logic; preserve it.
