# 1 Enoch — book context for review and drafting

- **Work**: 1 Enoch / Ethiopic Enoch / Mashafa Henok
- **Form**: composite apocalyptic work made of multiple earlier sections
- **Likely date**: roughly 3rd century BC to 1st century AD across its component parts
- **Primary surviving complete witness**: Ethiopic (Ge'ez)
- **Secondary witnesses**: partial Greek fragments (Panopolitanus, Syncellus, Chester Beatty) and Qumran Aramaic fragments for many sections except the Parables
- **Likely original audience**: Jews shaped by apocalyptic, priestly, and wisdom traditions in the Second Temple period
- **Translation posture**: treat the Ge'ez as the base text, but stay alert to places where Greek/Qumran evidence clarifies a difficult reading

## What matters most for translation

1. **This is visionary and apocalyptic prose/poetry.** Do not flatten its cosmic imagery into generic modern religious language.
2. **The book is composite.** The tone of the Watchers, Parables, Astronomical Book, Dream Visions, and Epistle can differ; preserve those shifts.
3. **Do not over-harmonize with later Christian usage.** Terms like “chosen ones,” “righteous,” “holy ones,” “Son of Man,” judgment, and enthronement should be rendered from the local passage first, not from later doctrinal expectations.
4. **The Parables (chs. 37–71) are Ge'ez-only in practice for this workflow.** That means difficult lines there deserve extra restraint and transparency.
5. **Where Jude or other familiar texts echo Enoch, preserve Enoch’s own wording and rhetoric rather than back-porting canonical phrasing.**

## Style / register

- Aim for elevated but readable English.
- Preserve the force of visionary reports, angelic speech, woes, blessings, and judgment scenes.
- Keep repeated apocalyptic formulas repeated.
- When the Ge'ez wording is uncertain, prefer transparent English plus a footnote over false confidence.
