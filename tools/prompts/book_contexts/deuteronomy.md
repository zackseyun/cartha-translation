# Deuteronomy — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 34 chapters.
- **Form**: Farewell speeches of Moses structured as a covenant document (preamble, historical review, law, blessing and curse, succession). Rhetorical and hortatory throughout.
- **Date**: Final form 7th–6th cent. BC; the core ("Deuteronomic") law code (12–26) may be connected to Josiah's reform (621 BC).
- **Original audience**: Israel on the plains of Moab, addressed as "you" — the rhetoric binds every generation of readers into the covenant.

## Key translation issues

1. **שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל** (6:4 — "Hear, O Israel"): The Shema. "The LORD our God, the LORD is one." Keep "hear" — it is a command, not just "listen." Render carefully; this is the central creed of Judaism.
2. **אָהַב** ("love"): Deuteronomy uses covenant-love language throughout — "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart…" (6:5). In covenantal context, "love" includes loyalty and obedience, not merely emotion; still render "love."
3. **לֵבָב / לֵב** ("heart"): Often "heart and soul and might" (6:5). "Heart" is correct; do not substitute "mind" for לֵב though Hebrew "heart" often means what English calls "mind."
4. **The repeated hortatory formulas**: "That it may go well with you," "that you may live long in the land," "as the LORD your God commanded you" — these are formulaic and must be rendered consistently throughout.
5. **גֵּר / נָכְרִי** ("sojourner/resident alien / foreigner"): Deuteronomy distinguishes these. גֵּר is a resident alien living among Israel (to be protected); נָכְרִי is a foreigner (different legal status). Maintain the distinction.
6. **נָבִיא** ("prophet"): 18:15–18 — "The LORD your God will raise up for you a prophet like me." The singular/collective ambiguity is intentional. Render literally.
7. **בָּרוּךְ / אָרוּר** (chs. 27–28 — "blessed / cursed"): The covenant blessings and curses. Keep "blessed" and "cursed" — do not soften "cursed" to "condemned."

## Style / register

- Sustained hortatory rhetoric. Moses speaks directly and urgently to "you."
- The law code (12–26) alternates between casuistic law ("if a man does X, then…") and direct address ("you shall not…"). Preserve both modes.
- The Song of Moses (32:1–43) and Blessing of Moses (33) are poetry — render as verse with appropriate elevation.

## Known pitfalls

- **Losing the rhetorical urgency** of the second-person address. "You" is singular and direct throughout much of the book.
- **Inconsistency in the repeated formulas** — they must be rendered identically every time they appear.
- **Rendering 18:15 as clearly messianic** in the translation text itself. Translate; let footnotes handle the interpretation.
