# Amos — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 9 chapters.
- **Form**: Prophetic oracles — mostly poetry. Oracles against the nations (1–2), judgment speeches (3–6), visions (7–9), closing restoration oracle.
- **Date**: ca. 760–750 BC. A southern prophet (from Tekoa in Judah) sent to the northern kingdom.
- **Original audience**: The prosperous but unjust northern kingdom of Israel.

## Key translation issues

1. **The oracles against the nations** (1:3–2:16): Each nation's indictment begins "For three transgressions of X, and for four, I will not revoke the punishment." The formula is identical each time. "For three transgressions… and for four" is the graded numerical saying; render consistently.
2. **מִשְׁפָּט / צְדָקָה** (5:24 — "justice / righteousness"): "Let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream." One of the most famous OT verses. Keep "justice" and "righteousness" as distinct — do not merge or vary.
3. **"Seek the LORD and live"** (5:4, 6, 14): The repeated call to repentance. Keep "seek" — not "look to" or "turn to."
4. **The visions** (7–9): Five vision reports, each beginning "This is what the Lord GOD showed me…" Keep the formula consistent.
5. **אֲדֹנָי יְהוִה** ("Lord GOD / Sovereign LORD"): Amos's characteristic divine title. The combination of אֲדֹנָי (Lord) + YHWH — render "the Lord GOD" (not "Lord God") to signal the special combination.
6. **The social critique language**: "Selling the righteous for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals" (2:6); "cows of Bashan" (4:1). Keep the concrete, vivid imagery — do not abstract it.
7. **The doxologies** (4:13; 5:8–9; 9:5–6): Hymnic fragments praising YHWH as creator. Render as elevated hymn poetry.

## Style / register

- Sharp, direct, satirical. Amos is a farmer who speaks with prophetic bluntness.
- The social justice language is concrete and specific — names, commodities, places.
- The rhythm of the repeated formulas must be preserved.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying the oracle formula** ("for three transgressions… and for four") — it must be identical.
- **Softening the social critique** — Amos is deliberately offensive to the comfortable.
- **Losing the doxologies' hymnic register.**
