# Matthew — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 28 chapters.
- **Form**: Gospel narrative with five major discourse blocks (Sermon on the Mount 5–7, Mission Discourse 10, Parables 13, Community Discourse 18, Eschatological Discourse 24–25). Framed by genealogy (1:1–17) and Great Commission (28:18–20).
- **Date**: ca. 80–90 AD. Post-Temple-destruction context.
- **Original audience**: Jewish Christian community, probably in Antioch or Syria. The book addresses questions of continuity with Torah and the identity of Jesus as fulfillment.

## Key translation issues

1. **"Kingdom of heaven" (βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν) vs. "Kingdom of God"**: Matthew uses "heaven" as a circumlocution for the divine name, following Jewish convention. Always render literally "kingdom of heaven" — do not harmonize to Mark/Luke's "kingdom of God."
2. **δικαιοσύνη** ("righteousness"): Matthew's key term (5:6, 10, 20; 6:1, 33; 21:32). Render consistently as "righteousness" — not "justice" (which loses the personal covenant dimension) and not "right living" (which is too behavioral and narrow).
3. **The fulfillment formulas** ("in order to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet…"): the repeated citation formula must be rendered consistently. The word "fulfill" (πληρόω) is theologically loaded — do not vary.
4. **μακάριος** (Beatitudes, 5:3–12): "Blessed" is the traditional rendering. "Happy" (NAB) is too flat. "Blessed" — the word describes a condition recognized by God, not a subjective feeling.
5. **Sermon on the Mount antitheses** (5:21–48): "You have heard that it was said… But I say to you…" Keep the exact antithetical formula consistently across all six antitheses.
6. **πατὴρ ἡμῶν ὁ ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς** ("our Father in heaven"): Matthew's characteristic address. Keep "our Father in heaven" — not "our Father who is in heaven" (the shorter form is closer to the Greek).
7. **ἔθνη** (28:19 — "nations"): "all nations" — not "Gentiles" (which carries a more restricted ethnic sense). The Great Commission is universal.
8. **εὐαγγέλιον** ("gospel / good news"): "the gospel" or "the good news" — context determines which is more natural.

## Style / register

- Narrative is direct and economical; Matthew is a disciplined narrator.
- The discourse blocks are elevated didactic speech.
- Parables use simple, concrete, vivid language — preserve the simplicity.
- The passion narrative (26–27) is terse and controlled despite its emotional weight.

## Known pitfalls

- **Harmonizing Matthew to Mark or Luke.** Render Matthew's own wording precisely.
- **Over-rendering δικαιοσύνη** as "justification" (the Pauline technical sense) — Matthew uses it as a broader character term.
- **Weakening the antithetical formula** (5:21–48). The "but I say to you" must be heard as a strong contrast.
