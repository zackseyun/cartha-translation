# Ecclesiastes — book context for review

- **Hebrew title**: קֹהֶלֶת (*Qoheleth*) — "the one who assembles / gathers."
  The Greek Ἐκκλησιαστής is a translational gloss; the Hebrew stays
  semantically open and we transliterate rather than resolve it.
- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT).
- **Form**: Wisdom literature, composite voice. A framing narrator
  introduces and closes the book (1:1–2; 12:9–14); inside the frame,
  Qoheleth speaks in first person about what he has observed "under
  the sun." Prose alternates with quoted aphorism; a few passages
  rise into formal poetry (3:1–8; 11:9–12:7).
- **Date / setting**: Post-exilic Jerusalem, most commonly placed
  late Persian to early Hellenistic (5th–3rd c. BC). The Hebrew
  bears late features and loanwords (Aramaic; possibly Persian) and
  wrestles with the disjunction between inherited wisdom confidence
  and observed reality.
- **Original audience**: Jewish readers inside a wisdom tradition
  that taught, after Proverbs, that righteous diligence secures
  flourishing. Qoheleth writes to an audience *steeped* in that
  claim and is checking it against what he actually sees.

## The governing motif — הֶבֶל (*hevel*)

This is the single word the entire book pivots on. It appears **38
times** in Ecclesiastes (out of 73 in the Hebrew Bible). Any verse
that contains it — or comments on what it names — is the hinge of
the book's argument, not incidental vocabulary. Getting *hevel*
right in the main text is the single most consequential lexical
decision for Ecclesiastes.

The concrete sense is *breath* or *vapor* (cf. Isaiah 57:13, Psalm
62:10/11, Proverbs 21:6). Qoheleth uses it metaphorically, and the
metaphor carries **three linked senses at once**, not one:

1. **Insubstantial / fleeting** — the thing won't stay; try to grip
   it and it's already gone. (Like breath on a cold morning.)
2. **Elusive / ungraspable** — the phenomenon can't be caught,
   comprehended, or pinned down. The observer reaches for it and
   comes back empty-handed.
3. **Ironic / absurd** — an expectation built on wisdom's premises
   collides with what actually happens, leaving a mismatch the
   observer can't resolve.

Modern scholarly treatments that informed this guidance include
Seow (Anchor, 1997 — *"ephemeral, unreliable, incomprehensible"*),
Fox (*A Time to Tear Down, a Time to Build Up*, 1999 — argues for
"absurd"), Bartholomew (*Ecclesiastes*, BCOT, 2009), Crenshaw
(*Ecclesiastes*, OTL, 1987), and Alter (*The Wisdom Books*, 2010 —
prefers concrete "mere breath"). No single English word covers all
three senses; each candidate emphasizes a different one and drops
the others.

### Ranking the candidates against author intent

- **"breath" / "mere breath"** (Alter) — preserves the concrete
  image and keeps all three senses latent for the reader to
  develop. Matches the author's deliberate choice of an
  image-bearing noun over the abstract Hebrew alternatives he had
  available (שָׁוְא "worthless," רִיק "empty"). Superlative
  construct renders naturally as "breath of breaths" (Hebrew
  absolute-superlative). Predicate uses take "mere" for evaluative
  tone.
- **"fleeting" / "elusive" / "transient"** — preserves senses 1 and
  2 cleanly; sense 3 must be carried by context. Readable but
  abstracting — drops the concrete image Qoheleth chose.
- **"futile" / "futility"** — narrows to goal-frustration ("effort
  doesn't pay off"). Under-represents sense 1 (transience) and
  sense 2 (incomprehensibility) — both of which carry Qoheleth's
  most sustained arguments (3:11; 8:17 "no one can find out the
  work that is done under the sun").
- **"vanity"** (KJV/ESV/NRSV) — archaic for modern English readers,
  who hear "pride / conceit" first. Do not use in the main text;
  footnote it as the traditional English only.
- **"meaningless"** (NIV) — imports modern existentialist nihilism
  Qoheleth does not assert. The book closes (12:13–14) with the
  fear of God, not with the denial of meaning. Theological
  distortion.
- **"absurd"** (Fox) — defensible scholarship, but carries a
  20th-century Camus/Kierkegaard footprint that feels
  anachronistic in a 3rd-century-BC sage.

### Default recommendation

Preserve the concrete image — **"breath" in the Hebrew superlative
construct, "mere breath" in predicate position.**

- הֲבֵל הֲבָלִים → **"breath of breaths"** (1:2, 12:8). The
  superlative does its own intensifying work, the way "holy of
  holies" does. Do not force "mere" here — "mere breath of mere
  breaths" is absurd.
- הָבֶל / הֶבֶל in predicate position ("everything is X," "this
  also is X") → **"mere breath."** Without "mere," a line like
  *"this also is breath"* reads as an ontological claim about
  substance rather than Qoheleth's evaluative verdict. "Mere"
  supplies the surprise-and-deflation tone Hebrew readers pick up
  from context but English readers won't supply unprompted.

This matches the author's specific choice of a concrete-image word
and preserves all three senses (transient, elusive, ironic) latent
to the reader across all 38 occurrences, without forcing a single
abstracting gloss to carry the book.

The footnote on 1:2 (the opening refrain) is mandatory and
substantive: it names הֶבֶל, its literal meaning, its three-way
semantic range, and notes the book-wide rendering policy. This is
the place the reader is paused and paying attention; one strong
footnote does the anchoring work for the whole book so the main
text elsewhere can stay clean English.

"Fleeting" is acceptable as a contextual alternative in verses
where the concrete noun reads awkwardly (rare). "Futility" is
acceptable only in passages specifically complaining about the
reward-for-effort asymmetry (e.g., 2:11, 2:19, 4:7–8) — and even
there, push back toward the concrete image first.

## Other recurring vocabulary

- **"under the sun"** (תַּחַת הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, 29×): Qoheleth's technical
  frame for the empirical-observational domain. Always render
  literally "under the sun" — never "on earth" or "in this world."
  The phrase is doing structural work.
- **יִתְרוֹן** ("gain," "advantage," "profit"): a commercial-register
  term Qoheleth borrows. Render consistently as "gain" so the
  recurring question *mah-yitrōn lā-ādām* ("what gain has a person
  from…") registers as the same question across chapters.
- **עָמָל** ("toil," "labor"): carries a weight of wearying effort,
  not neutral "work." Keep "toil" where possible; only fall back to
  "work" when "toil" is grammatically awkward.
- **רְעוּת רוּחַ / רַעְיוֹן רוּחַ** ("a chasing after wind" / "a
  striving after wind"): Qoheleth's paired phrase with hevel. Keep
  the *chasing-after-wind* image intact — it's the kinetic
  companion to hevel's static-breath image.
- **חָכְמָה** ("wisdom"): Qoheleth uses this in the full Proverbs
  sense, then interrogates that sense. Don't soften to "knowledge."
- **עוֹלָם** (3:11 — "he has put עוֹלָם into their heart"): famously
  contested. Options: "eternity" (traditional), "a sense of time
  past and future" (NET), "the world" (JPS, following medieval
  Jewish commentary reading עֹלָם). Flag as a theological_decision
  and footnote the alternatives; do not quietly pick one.

## Style / register

- Qoheleth's voice is reflective, world-weary, and unblinking. The
  English should read like a wise observer thinking out loud, not
  like a preacher.
- Where he cites or echoes Proverbs-style aphorism (e.g., 7:1–12),
  let the aphorism sound aphoristic; where he undercuts it, the
  undercut has to land. The tonal shift is part of the argument.
- Do not smooth the internal tensions. Qoheleth contradicts
  himself (2:24 vs 2:17; 7:3 vs 7:9) on purpose — those collisions
  are the point. Don't harmonize.
- Preserve rhetorical questions as questions in English.
- The closing frame (12:9–14) speaks *about* Qoheleth in a
  different voice from Qoheleth; keep that shift audible.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening hevel to one English word across the book.** The
  book argues with a multivalent term; a single English gloss
  defaults to whichever sense the translator finds most legible,
  and the rest of the senses drop out of the reader's hearing.
  Prefer a per-context decision with a footnote on the first
  occurrence of the refrain.
- **Translating 2:24–26 or 3:12–13 as straight-up endorsement.**
  These carpe-diem passages are *provisional* conclusions inside a
  larger argument — not the book's final word. Preserve the
  tentativeness the Hebrew carries.
- **Over-reading "fear God" (12:13) back into earlier chapters.**
  The frame narrator's summary reinterprets what Qoheleth has said;
  don't let 12:13's reverent register colonize the English of the
  chapters where Qoheleth is not yet at that register.
- **Rendering קֹהֶלֶת as "the Preacher" or "the Teacher."** Both
  interpretive; both narrow. Transliterate as "Qoheleth" and
  footnote the alternatives, matching DOCTRINE.md's transliterate-
  when-uncertain principle.
