# Joel — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for variants.
- **Form**: Prophetic poetry interspersed with prose calls to lamentation. Three chapters (Hebrew: two in some traditions). Two main sections: locust plague / drought (1:1–2:17) and divine response / eschatological promise (2:18–3:21).
- **Date**: Debated widely. No Assyria, Babylon, or Persia mentioned — may be early (pre-exilic) or post-exilic. Most contemporary scholars lean post-exilic (5th–4th c. BC) based on the advanced eschatological theology.
- **Original audience**: Judah. The immediate crisis is a devastating locust plague; the prophet reads it as a harbinger of "the Day of the LORD."

## Key translation issues

1. **יוֹם יהוה** ("the Day of the LORD"): Joel's central concept. Appears at 1:15; 2:1, 11, 31; 3:14. Always render literally "the Day of the LORD" — not "the day of judgment" or "the great day." The term is a technical prophetic idiom; its weight depends on keeping it stable across all occurrences.
2. **אַרְבֶּה / גָּזָם / יֶלֶק / חָסִיל** (1:4 — the four stages/species of locust): the identification of these terms is disputed; they may refer to different locust stages or different species. Do not collapse them to "locusts" four times — the Hebrew distinguishes four terms and the text's four-fold horror is part of the rhetorical force. Keep all four distinct, even if footnotes are needed.
3. **הַיּוֹם הַגָּדוֹל וְהַנּוֹרָא** (2:31 — "the great and terrible day"): eschatological climax. Keep "terrible" (awesome / fearsome) — not "awesome" alone, which in modern English has lost its fear-weight.
4. **שָׁפַכְתִּי אֶת-רוּחִי** (2:28 — "I will pour out my Spirit"): cited in Acts 2:17. Render "my Spirit" (uppercase) consistently — this is the divine ruach in its eschatological fullness.
5. **שׁוּב** / repentance calls (2:12–13): "Return to me with all your heart." Keep the directness of the imperative address.

## Style / register

- Prophetic poetry; the lament sections have genuine urgency and grief.
- The call to lamentation (1:5–14) addresses different social groups in sequence; the escalating address is rhetorical and should be preserved.
- The divine speech sections (2:19–27; 2:28–32; 3:1–21) shift register to direct-address oracle; keep the first-person divine voice prominent.

## Known pitfalls

- **Conflating the four locust terms at 1:4.** This sacrifices deliberate rhetorical structure.
- **Weakening "the Day of the LORD" with paraphrase.** The eschatological weight depends on the literal phrase.
- **Missing the literary seam at 2:18** where the book shifts from crisis to promise; the shift in verbal tense (imperfect consecutive as prophetic perfect) can be ambiguous — render with a footnote if needed.
