# Sirach (Ecclesiasticus / Ben Sira) — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Ziegler critical edition). Hebrew Geniza fragments (Cairo Geniza) and Masada scroll for text-critical comparison where available. 51 chapters + prologue.
- **Form**: Wisdom literature. Extended collection of maxims, poems, hymns (especially the Praise of Famous Men, chs. 44–50), and wisdom poems. More voluminous than Proverbs.
- **Author / date**: Joshua ben Sira (Jesus son of Sirach), early 2nd c. BC, Jerusalem. Translated into Greek by his grandson (Alexandria, ca. 132 BC per the Prologue).
- **Original audience**: Upper-class Jerusalem Jews engaged in commerce, diplomacy, and scribal work. Ben Sira is a professional wisdom teacher.

## Key translation issues

1. **Greek vs. Hebrew text**: Where the Cairo Geniza Hebrew is available, it often differs from the LXX (the Greek often harmonizes or softens). The COB uses LXX as base text. Do not import readings from the Hebrew without noting it.
2. **σοφία** ("wisdom"): central throughout. Wisdom as a hypostatic figure (ch. 24) — "Lady Wisdom" — is the book's theological climax. Keep "Wisdom" capitalized when personified in ch. 24; be consistent throughout.
3. **ἐλεημοσύνη** ("almsgiving / mercy"): in Sirach this means specifically charitable giving to the poor, not just mercy in a general sense. Render as "almsgiving" in contexts where giving to the poor is explicit (7:10; 12:3; 17:22; 29:12; 40:24; 50:24).
4. **δόξα** ("glory / honor"): appears in both divine and human registers. Context determines which — "glory" for divine doxology, "honor" when applied to human reputation.
5. **The Praise of Famous Men** (chs. 44–50): an anaphoric structure built on "Let us now praise famous men…" Preserve the anaphoric rhythm; the repeated "Also…" / "And there was…" pattern is structural.
6. **Simon the High Priest** (ch. 50): Ben Sira's contemporary and the climax of the Praise. Render the temple ceremony description with appropriate liturgical register.
7. **Prologue**: the grandson's Prologue explains the translation limitations. Render it carefully — it is a key text about translation theory.

## Style / register

- The dominant register is practical-wisdom: direct, experienced, aphoristic.
- Hymnic sections (1:1–10; 24:1–22; 42:15–43:33; 51:1–12) lift into elevated poetry.
- The Praise of Famous Men is formal and elevated throughout.
- Household and social instruction (chs. 3–4, 7, 22, 25–26) has a personal-advisory register.

## Known pitfalls

- **Inconsistent rendering of σοφία** — not always keeping the feminine personification when the text uses it.
- **Translating ἐλεημοσύνη as "mercy"** in contexts that clearly mean charitable giving.
- **Flattening the chapter 24 Wisdom poem** into prose register. It is high poetry; render it accordingly.
- **Losing the anaphoric structure of the Praise of Famous Men.** The "and also" / "he was" pattern is rhythmic and must be preserved.
