# Judges — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 21 chapters.
- **Form**: Narrative — the cycles of Israel's apostasy, oppression, cry for deliverance, and rescue by a "judge" (military deliverer). Ends in increasingly dark narratives (17–21).
- **Date**: Final deuteronomistic editing 7th–6th cent. BC.
- **Original audience**: Israel's pre-monarchic era — the "judges" (שֹׁפְטִים) cycle demonstrates the need for faithful covenant leadership.

## Key translation issues

1. **שֹׁפֵט** ("judge"): The book's title word. In Judges, שֹׁפֵט means military deliverer and regional leader, not primarily a judicial officer. Keep "judge" as the traditional rendering — footnote the broader meaning.
2. **The cycle formula**: "The Israelites did evil in the sight of the LORD" → oppression → "they cried out to the LORD" → deliverer raised up → "the land had rest X years." Render each element of the formula consistently and identically.
3. **רוּחַ יְהוָה** ("the Spirit of the LORD"): "The Spirit of the LORD came upon him" — standard formula for divine empowerment of the judges. Keep "Spirit of the LORD" — not "the wind of God."
4. **The Song of Deborah** (5:1–31): One of the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible — archaic poetry. Render as verse with full poetic force. Preserve the archaic flavor rather than modernizing.
5. **Gideon's fleece test** (6:36–40), **Samson's riddles** (14:14, 18): Keep the folk-tale quality. Do not over-literalize or over-explain.
6. **"In those days there was no king in Israel; everyone did what was right in his own eyes"** (17:6; 21:25): The Deuteronomistic editorial refrain. Keep exactly — both occurrences must be word-for-word identical.
7. **The dark endings** (chs. 17–21: Micah's idols, Levite's concubine, war against Benjamin): Render the violence and moral darkness without sanitizing.

## Style / register

- Narrative is vivid, episodic, sometimes darkly ironic (Ehud, Jephthah's vow, Samson).
- The Song of Deborah is archaic Hebrew poetry — notoriously difficult; footnote uncertain readings.
- The final chapters (17–21) have a deliberate bleakness.

## Known pitfalls

- **Softening the violence** in chapters 19–21 — the text's darkness is the point.
- **Varying the cycle formula** across chapters — it must be formulaically consistent.
- **Missing "in his own eyes"** (21:25) — a precise phrase, not just "as they saw fit."
