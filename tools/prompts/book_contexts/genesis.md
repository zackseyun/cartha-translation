# Genesis — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) and Samaritan Pentateuch for text-critical reference. DSS fragments (4QGen) where relevant.
- **Form**: Narrative + genealogy + poetry. Primeval history (1–11) + Patriarchal narratives (12–50). 50 chapters.
- **Date**: Final form likely post-exilic (Priestly redaction), incorporating earlier sources (J, E, P, D debates ongoing). For translation purposes, render the final MT text.
- **Original audience**: Israel/Judah; the book establishes covenant identity, land promises, and the origins of the people of God.

## Key translation issues

1. **בְּרֵאשִׁית** (1:1): "In the beginning" is the standard rendering. The Hebrew is ambiguous on whether this is an absolute statement ("In the beginning God created…") or a temporal clause ("When God began to create…"). The MT punctuation (no rebia on בְּרֵאשִׁית) slightly favors the absolute reading, but do not over-determine; footnote the ambiguity.
2. **אָדָם**: the generic "humankind / humanity" (1:26–27) vs. the personal name "Adam" (4:25; 5:1). The shift from generic to personal is important; context must govern the rendering, and the distinction must be audible.
3. **צֶלֶם / דְּמוּת** (1:26–27 — "image / likeness"): the *imago Dei* passage. "Image and likeness" is the traditional rendering; "image" and "likeness" are best kept distinct (צֶלֶם and דְּמוּת are different words). Do not collapse to one English word.
4. **תֹּהוּ וָבֹהוּ** (1:2 — "formless and void / waste and empty"): a fixed alliterative pair. Render with two parallel English terms; the alliteration is part of the effect ("formless and void" or "waste and empty").
5. **בְּרִית** ("covenant"): first appears at 6:18 (Noah) and recurs through the Abrahamic and patriarchal narratives. Render consistently as "covenant" — this is the key relational-legal term of the Pentateuch.
6. **חֶסֶד** in patriarchal narratives (19:19; 20:13; 21:23; 24:27; etc.): "steadfast love / loyalty / kindness." Render with attention to the covenantal freight.
7. **יהוה vs. אֱלֹהִים**: The divine name יהוה should be rendered "the LORD" (small caps) consistently; אֱלֹהִים as "God." The name-use patterns are source-critical data; do not obscure them.

## The Primeval History (chs. 1–11) — specific issues

- **The creation accounts** (1:1–2:3 and 2:4–25): different names for God, different style. Keep the stylistic distinction.
- **The "sons of God" / בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים** (6:1–4): contested — divine beings or humans? Keep "sons of God" (the literal Hebrew) and footnote the interpretation question.
- **נֶפֶשׁ חַיָּה** (2:7 — "living being / soul"): the same phrase is used for animals (1:20, 24). Render 2:7 as "living being" — not "immortal soul." The word applies to all creatures.

## Style / register

- The priestly material (1:1–2:3; genealogies) has formal, rhythmic, liturgical prose. Keep it.
- The narrative material (2:4–11; 12–50) is more earthy, dialogic, and character-driven.
- Poetry appears at 2:23 (first human speech as poetry — keep it as verse), 49:1–27 (Jacob's blessing — formal tribal poetry).

## Known pitfalls

- **Reading the creation days through modern cosmology or evolutionary debate.** Translate what the text says.
- **Psychologizing the patriarchal characters beyond what the text supports.** The narratives are often deliberately laconic; preserve the gaps.
- **Translating אָדָם as "Adam" in 1:26–27** before the word functions as a personal name.
