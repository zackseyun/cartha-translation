# 1 Samuel — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 31 chapters.
- **Form**: Narrative — the transition from judges to monarchy. Three main sections: Samuel (1–7), Saul's rise and fall (8–15), David's rise alongside Saul's decline (16–31).
- **Date**: Deuteronomistic final editing 7th–6th cent. BC; core narratives much older.
- **Original audience**: Israel's memory of how kingship began and the theological tensions it created.

## Key translation issues

1. **מָשִׁיחַ** ("anointed one / messiah"): Saul and David are both called "the LORD's anointed" (מְשִׁיחַ יְהוָה). Render "the LORD's anointed" — not "the Messiah" (which imports the later specialized meaning). Footnote the connection.
2. **חֶסֶד** ("steadfast love / loyalty / kindness"): Appears in David's interactions with Jonathan and others (20:14–15). Render with the covenant dimension — "steadfast love" or "loyalty."
3. **Hannah's prayer** (2:1–10): A psalm of reversal closely related to the Magnificat. Render as poetry. The exclamatory "My heart exults in the LORD" — not "My heart is happy."
4. **The ark narrative** (4–6): The ark as the embodied presence of God. The ark's capture, journey among the Philistines, and return are told with dark humor and theological weight. Preserve the narrative irony.
5. **"The LORD regretted…"** (15:11, 35 — וַיִּנָּחֶם יְהוָה): The theological problem of divine repentance. Render "the LORD regretted" — not "the LORD changed his mind" (too casual). Footnote the anthropomorphism.
6. **David and Goliath** (17): Preserve the storytelling rhythm. The challenge formula, David's speeches, and the battle sequence all have a particular cadence.
7. **The narrative of Saul's decline**: Saul's psychological deterioration — preserve the ambiguity in the text; the narrator does not moralize excessively.

## Style / register

- Excellent narrative prose — vivid characterization, dialogue, dramatic tension.
- Hannah's prayer is elevated poetry; preserve the register shift.
- Court narrative (David in Saul's service) has a subtle, oblique quality.

## Known pitfalls

- **Capitalizing "Anointed" / "Messiah"** in Saul's references — the term applies to Saul too, not only David; do not make it implicitly Christological in the text.
- **Flattening "the LORD regretted"** — the anthropomorphism should remain.
