# 1 Kings — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 22 chapters.
- **Form**: Narrative — Solomon's reign (1–11), the divided kingdom, prophets Elijah and Elisha cycle begins.
- **Date**: Deuteronomistic editing 7th–6th cent. BC.
- **Original audience**: Israel's history of the monarchy — why the kingdom split and fell.

## Key translation issues

1. **Solomon's prayer at temple dedication** (8:22–53): Extended royal prayer of great formal dignity. Render with appropriate elevation. The repeated formula "hear in heaven your dwelling place" must be consistent.
2. **"Walking in the ways of David"** and **"doing evil in the sight of the LORD"**: The Deuteronomistic evaluation formula for each king. Every king receives this verdict — keep the exact wording identical across all occurrences.
3. **The Elijah narratives** (17–19; 21): Vivid narrative with a strong folk-tale quality. Key scenes: the widow of Zarephath, Elijah on Carmel, the still small voice (or "sound of thin silence" / "gentle whisper") at Horeb.
4. **קוֹל דְּמָמָה דַקָּה** (19:12 — "a still small voice / a sound of sheer silence / a gentle whisper"): The phrase after the wind and earthquake. Literally "the sound of a thin/fine silence." "Still small voice" (KJV) is traditional; "sound of sheer silence" (NRSV) is more literally precise. Footnote the options.
5. **בַּמוֹת** ("high places"): The sites of local worship outside Jerusalem. "High places" is the standard rendering — do not substitute "shrines" (which loses the geographical specificity).
6. **Elijah's dramatic speech at Carmel** (18:21): "How long will you waver between two opinions?" — the challenge to the people. Render with rhetorical sharpness.

## Style / register

- Royal annals style in the framework; vivid narrative in the Elijah cycle.
- Solomon's temple dedication prayer: formal, liturgical, elevated.
- Elijah's story: folk-tale energy, dramatic confrontation, psychological depth.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying the regnal evaluation formula** — it must be verbatim identical across all kings.
- **Over-interpreting "high places"** as inherently evil in the translation — let the narrative provide context.
