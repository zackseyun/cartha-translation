# Proverbs — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 31 chapters.
- **Form**: Wisdom poetry. Collections of sayings attributed to Solomon plus other sages (Agur 30:1–9; Lemuel's mother 31:1–9). Opens with an extended instruction section (1–9) including the Woman Wisdom and Woman Folly personifications.
- **Date**: Compiled post-exilic; core collections may be pre-exilic.
- **Original audience**: Young men being formed in wisdom — the book's rhetoric is a father instructing a son.

## Key translation issues

1. **חָכְמָה** ("wisdom"): The central value of the book. Always "wisdom" — not "skill," "cleverness," or "understanding" (those are separate terms). Note that wisdom in Proverbs is both practical competence and moral orientation.
2. **בִּינָה / שֶׂכֶל / דַּעַת** (understanding / insight / knowledge): These wisdom terms are near-synonyms but distinct. Establish stable renderings and keep them.
3. **Woman Wisdom (חָכְמָה) and Woman Folly** (chs. 8–9): Wisdom is personified as a woman who calls out in the public square; Folly is her counterpart. Render these as personified figures, not abstractions. In 8:22–31, Wisdom's primordial role beside God must be rendered carefully — "the beginning of his work" (or "the first of his acts") and "beside him, like a master workman."
4. **אָמוֹן** (8:30 — "master workman / little child / nursling / confidant"): One of the most contested words in Proverbs. The meaning is genuinely uncertain. Footnote the alternatives; pick the most defensible ("master workman" or "craftsman" is widely supported).
5. **The parallelism structure**: Hebrew proverbs operate through synonymous, antithetical, or synthetic parallelism. The English must preserve the two-part structure. Do not collapse both halves into one sentence.
6. **יְהוָה** ("the LORD"): Many proverbs directly invoke the LORD — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7). Always "the LORD"; never dilute to "God."
7. **Chapters 10–29 (individual proverbs)**: Each saying stands alone. Do not create artificial connections between adjacent sayings that the Hebrew does not imply.

## Style / register

- Aphoristic, crisp, memorable. A good proverb translation should be quotable.
- Chapters 1–9: extended addresses from father to son; the rhetoric is instructional and warm.
- Chapters 10–22: terse antithetical proverbs ("A wise son makes a glad father, but a foolish son is a sorrow to his mother").
- The capable wife poem (31:10–31) is an acrostic — render it as poetry.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening the parallelism** into single sentences — the two-part structure is the form.
- **Over-spiritualizing wisdom** away from its practical, everyday dimension (wisdom includes good judgment in business, relationships, speech).
- **Rendering אָמוֹן (8:30) without a footnote.** The options matter theologically and the text is genuinely contested.
