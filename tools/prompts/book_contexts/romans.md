# Romans — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 16 chapters.
- **Form**: Pauline letter — the most systematic theological exposition in the NT. Structured as a sustained argument with diatribe elements (imagined objectors).
- **Date**: ca. 57 AD from Corinth, during Paul's third missionary journey.
- **Original audience**: The Roman church (mixed Jewish and Gentile), which Paul had not yet visited.

## Key translation issues

1. **δικαιοσύνη / δικαιόω / δίκαιος** ("righteousness / to justify / righteous"): the most critical word cluster in the letter. In Paul's usage it carries forensic (legal acquittal) and covenant (right relationship) dimensions. The entire argument of 1:16–8:39 turns on this vocabulary. Render consistently: δικαιοσύνη = "righteousness," δικαιόω = "to justify," δίκαιος = "righteous." Do not substitute "right," "upright," or "vindicate" without a compelling reason; footnote any departure.
2. **πίστις / πιστεύω** ("faith / to believe / faithfulness"): the πίστις Χριστοῦ debate (3:22, 26; Galatians 2:16) — "faith of Christ" (subjective genitive: Christ's own faithfulness) vs. "faith in Christ" (objective genitive: our faith directed at Christ). Both are grammatically possible. Do not resolve silently; render and footnote.
3. **νόμος** ("law"): Paul uses "law" in multiple senses — Mosaic Torah, the law as a principle, the law of faith, the law of sin and death. Context must govern; when Paul switches senses within a passage (e.g., 7:21–25), the switch must be audible in English.
4. **σάρξ** ("flesh"): Paul's specialized term for the unredeemed human tendency apart from the Spirit (not simply "the body"). "Flesh" is more accurate than "sinful nature" (NIV) — the latter is an interpretation, not a translation. "The flesh" should be kept throughout Romans 7–8.
5. **ἱλαστήριον** (3:25 — "propitiation / expiation / mercy seat"): all three renderings are defensible; each carries a different theological emphasis. Render with the most defensible translation and footnote the alternatives. "Sacrifice of atonement" (NRSV) is a paraphrase; the single Greek word is more specific.
6. **λογίζομαι** ("to reckon / count / credit"): the term in 4:3–11 (crediting Abraham's faith as righteousness). "Credited" or "reckoned" — not "counted" alone (which is too informal).
7. **Chapters 9–11** (election, Israel, salvation): the theological argument is dense and precise. Every predestination/election term (ἐκλογή, προορίζω, προγινώσκω, καλέω) must be rendered accurately and consistently.

## Style / register

- Sustained theological argument; the register is elevated and precise.
- Diatribe sections (2:1–5, 2:17–29; 3:1–9; 9:14–21) involve rhetorical interlocutors — preserve the dialogical format.
- Hymnic sections (8:31–39; 11:33–36) lift into elevated prose-poetry; render with that register.

## Known pitfalls

- **Translating σάρξ as "sinful nature."** This is interpretation, not translation.
- **Silently resolving the πίστις Χριστοῦ debate.** Footnote it.
- **Losing the consistency of the δικαιοσύνη word family.** The argument depends on readers hearing the same word root throughout.
