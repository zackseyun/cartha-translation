# Gospel of Truth — book context for review and drafting

- **Work**: Gospel of Truth (*Evangelium Veritatis*, usually abbreviated *GTr* or *GoT*)
- **Form**: A Valentinian Christian homily or meditation — NOT a narrative gospel. Roughly 28 pages in the Nag Hammadi codex. Commonly treated as 16 thematic sections.
- **Language**: Coptic — **Lycopolitan (Subakhmimic) dialect**, NOT Sahidic. This is critically different from Gospel of Thomas (NHC II,2 Sahidic). Morphology, vocabulary, and some verbal patterns diverge from the standard Sahidic that Crum primarily indexes.
- **Primary witness**: Nag Hammadi Codex I,3 (the Jung Codex), pp. 16.31–43.24, discovered 1945, published 1956 (Malinine/Puech/Quispel)
- **Secondary witness**: NHC XII,2 — badly preserved Sahidic-dialect fragments of the same work, surviving only for a portion
- **Date of composition**: c. 140–180 AD, likely in Rome or Alexandria, within Valentinian circles. Some scholars attribute it to Valentinus himself, though this is disputed.
- **Primary reference translations**:
  - **Mark M. Mattison, 2017** — public-domain (gospels.net/truth). Our staged reference.
  - Attridge & MacRae, *Nag Hammadi Studies* vols 22–23 (1985) — scholarly standard, copyrighted.
  - Layton, *The Gnostic Scriptures* (1987), Meyer, *The Nag Hammadi Scriptures* (2007) — scholarly, copyrighted.
- **Our source artifact**: Vertex Gemini 3.1 Pro facsimile OCR of NHC I,3 pages 16–43, with the NHC XII,2 fragments listed in source.primary_page_texts.
- **Original audience**: educated Christian Gnostics (Valentinians), steeped in Greek rhetoric and Middle Platonist vocabulary, familiar with the emerging NT corpus.

## What matters most for translation

1. **This is a mystical HOMILY, not a gospel narrative.** No birth/ministry/death story arc. It meditates on the Father, the Son, error, gnosis, the Book of the Living, and the return to unity. Translation must preserve its contemplative, winding structure.
2. **Dialect matters for lexical decisions.**
   - Sahidic-trained reviewers reading Lycopolitan morphology may misparse verb prefixes. Circumstantial perfect ⲉⲩ-/ⲉⲥ- and relative forms can look different from what Crum's main entries show.
   - Cite Crum by entry head-word but flag when the Lycopolitan form diverges.
   - Layton's Coptic grammar and Kasser's Lycopolitan studies are the right secondary lexical authorities.
3. **Valentinian theological vocabulary has specific meanings** that are NOT interchangeable with Pauline or Johannine Greek:
   - **pleroma** (ⲡⲗⲏⲣⲱⲙⲁ): "the fullness" — the divine totality of aeons, not just metaphorical abundance.
   - **aeon** (ⲁⲓⲱⲛ): emanated divine being, NOT "age" or "era."
   - **Father** / ⲡⲓⲱⲧ: the ultimate source; always capitalize.
   - **Name** (ⲣⲉⲛ): the Father's ineffable Name that the Son bears and reveals — it is a load-bearing concept (pp. 38–40).
   - **Error** (ⲡⲗⲁⲛⲏ): personified in the text, not mere "mistake." She/it is an actor (pp. 17–18).
   - **the Living Book** / **Book of the Living**: the revelatory text; distinct from the New Testament.
4. **Echoes of NT language are abundant but appropriative, not derivative.**
   - Allusions to Matthew (lost sheep, the vineyard), John (Word/λόγος, Father/Son, light/darkness), Colossians (preserving all things). The author assumes familiarity.
   - Preserve Valentinian re-framings: the "Word" here is the Son who reveals the Father's Name; "gnosis" is salvific knowledge that returns one from deficiency to fullness.
5. **DO NOT harmonize with canonical Christianity.** The Father ≠ the Judge-God of the OT here (implicit anti-demiurgic overtone in some passages); the "Son" is the Father's self-revelation; the saved are those the Father has written in the Living Book "from the beginning."
6. **Manuscript lacunae are real.** Mark gaps honestly as `[ ]` in the English. Editorial restorations are `( )`. Do not silently fill in what the Coptic doesn't say.
7. **Poetic clause parallelism** permeates the text — bicola, tricola, chiasms. Preserve them audibly.

## Style / register

- Elevated, meditative English. Think of it as Christian mystical literature — closer in register to Pseudo-Dionysius or the Odes of Solomon than to the Synoptic gospels.
- Mattison's PD translation reads modern and accessible; Attridge/MacRae more scholarly-literal. We aim between the two — accessible but faithful to Valentinian technical vocabulary.
- Preserve the text's metaphors — jars of perfume, nightmares and awakening, the Father's paradise, the sheep with the missing member — without paraphrasing them into generic religious language.

## Known translation pitfalls

- Collapsing ⲡⲗⲏⲣⲱⲙⲁ to "fullness" without context can lose the technical sense.
- Translating ⲡⲗⲁⲛⲏ as "deception" or "mistake" misses her personification.
- "Gnosis" (ⲥⲟⲩⲛⲧ-): "knowledge" works only if the reader can hear the salvific, revelatory sense; footnote when necessary.
- "Name" — if translated consistently with a capital N, the Father's Name becomes tractable in English as the reified thing the Son bears.
- **Verse numbering**: our translation is organized by 16 sections matching the tractate's thematic divisions, NOT by Nag Hammadi codex line numbers. Reference translations may divide differently.
- P.Oxy. fragments are NOT relevant here (they belong to Thomas); do not confuse the two.
