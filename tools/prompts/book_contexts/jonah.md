# Jonah — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for variants.
- **Form**: Prophetic narrative — but unlike other prophetic books, this is almost entirely story with no oracles, plus a psalm (ch. 2) embedded in the narrative. Four short chapters.
- **Date**: Likely post-exilic (Persian period), though the narrative is set in the Assyrian period (8th c. BC). The satirical, self-critical tone toward Israelite nationalism is consistent with post-exilic reflection.
- **Original audience**: Israelite/Jewish readers who would recognize Jonah as a known prophet (2 Kings 14:25) and feel the irony of a prophet fleeing God's call, a pagan city repenting, and the prophet sulking over divine mercy.

## Key translation issues

1. **גָּדוֹל** ("great"): the word appears ~14 times. Nineveh is a "great city," there is a "great wind," "great fear," "great evil" — the repetition is structural and emphatic. Do not vary "great" for stylistic diversity; preserve the refrain.
2. **רָעָה** ("evil / disaster / wickedness"): the same Hebrew word applies to both human sin (1:2; 3:8) and divine calamity (3:10; 4:2). The double meaning is almost certainly intentional — keep it by choosing a translation strategy that lets both senses remain. "Harm" or "disaster" for the divine act; "evil/wickedness" for the human act — or footnote the overlap.
3. **שׁוּב** ("to turn / to return / to repent"): 3:8–10 — the Ninevites "turn" from their evil, and God "turns" from the threatened disaster. The word is the same. The structural parallelism matters.
4. **חֵן** at 4:6 ("relief / delight"): the gourd plant brings Jonah great joy (שָׂמַח). This sets up the contrast of 4:9–11; preserve the emotional register.
5. **The fish** (דָּג גָּדוֹל, "great fish"): not "whale." The text says "great fish." Do not harmonize with Matthew 12:40's "whale."

## The psalm (ch. 2)

- A thanksgiving psalm, not a petition. Jonah thanks God for deliverance *from inside the fish*, speaking as if already rescued (past-tense thanksgiving — a psalmic convention). Do not smooth the temporal paradox.
- Echoes many canonical Psalms; that intertextuality is deliberate.

## Style / register

- Narrative prose with ironic distance. The narrator never editorializes directly but the irony is unmistakable (the pagan sailors pray more devoutly than the prophet; the pagan city repents while the prophet sulks).
- Jonah's speech in 4:2–3 quotes Exodus 34:6 — a confession of divine character that Jonah seems to resent. Keep the liturgical-quotation register of that line.
- The book ends with a question (4:11); do not resolve the ambiguity.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening the structural repetition of גָּדוֹל and רָעָה.** The refrain-like repetition is part of the book's artistry.
- **Over-explaining the irony in the translation.** Let the narrative speak; do not add glosses.
- **Rendering the plant at 4:6 as "gourd" without noting the uncertainty** — the Hebrew קִיקָיוֹן is a hapax; "gourd," "castor-oil plant," and "vine" are all proposed.
