# 1 Corinthians — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 16 chapters.
- **Form**: Pauline letter. Responding to reports and questions from the Corinthian church. Covers divisions, sexual ethics, marriage, food offered to idols, worship practice, spiritual gifts, and resurrection.
- **Date**: ca. 53–54 AD from Ephesus.
- **Original audience**: The church at Corinth — a cosmopolitan, status-conscious Greco-Roman city.

## Key translation issues

1. **σταυρός / μωρία** (1:18 — "the cross / folly"): "The word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing." Keep "folly" — Paul is using a rhetorical reversal. "Foolishness" (many translations) is acceptable; "nonsense" is too casual.
2. **πνευματικός / ψυχικός / σαρκικός** ("spiritual / natural/soulish / fleshly"): The three-way anthropological distinction in chapters 2–3. ψυχικός (from ψυχή, "soul") is NOT "natural" in a neutral sense — Paul contrasts it with πνευματικός. "Natural person" (ESV) or "unspiritual person" (NRSV) — footnote the Greek.
3. **The spiritual gifts (χαρίσματα)** (chs. 12–14): γλῶσσαι = "tongues" (not "languages" — the glossolalia context is visionary speech), προφητεία = "prophecy," ἑρμηνεία γλωσσῶν = "interpretation of tongues." Keep the traditional "tongues" — changing to "languages" resolves a theological debate that the translation should not resolve.
4. **ἀγάπη** (ch. 13 — "love"): The "love chapter." Keep "love" throughout (not "charity" as in KJV). The attributes in 13:4–7 must be rendered vividly and precisely.
5. **The resurrection chapter** (ch. 15): σῶμα πνευματικόν / σῶμα ψυχικόν — "spiritual body / natural body" (15:44). The contrast is between two kinds of body, not body vs. non-body. Render both as "body."
6. **"In Christ" (ἐν Χριστῷ)**: Paul's characteristic formula. Always "in Christ" — not "in union with Christ" or "in the Messiah" (the latter imposes Messianic interpretation).
7. **μαρανα θα** (16:22 — "Our Lord, come" / "Come, Lord!"): Aramaic liturgical formula. Transliterate and translate. "Maranatha" is traditional; footnote both possible parsings (marana tha = "our Lord, come" vs. maran atha = "our Lord has come").

## Style / register

- Argumentative, dialogical, sometimes ironic ("You have become kings!"). Paul uses diatribe, sarcasm, and rhetorical questions.
- The love poem (ch. 13) is elevated lyric prose — render with appropriate beauty.
- Chapters 1–4 have sophisticated rhetorical structure; preserve the argument's logic.

## Known pitfalls

- **Translating γλῶσσαι as "languages"** — this pre-decides the debate about the gift's nature.
- **Flattening ψυχικός** to "natural" without noting the soul/spirit contrast.
- **Losing the irony** in chapters 1–4 ("not many of you were wise by worldly standards…").
