# Song of Songs — book context for review

- **Hebrew title**: שִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים (*Shir ha-Shirim*) — "the Song of Songs" (superlative: the greatest song).
- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for text-critical notes.
- **Form**: Love poetry, possibly an anthology of discrete poems or a loosely unified composition. Alternating voices (female beloved, male lover, "daughters of Jerusalem" chorus). No narrative frame; pure lyric.
- **Date**: Debated. Late Iron Age to Persian period; the Hebrew shows both early and late features. Placed among the Ketuvim.
- **Original audience**: Read in Israelite/Jewish wisdom tradition; later allegorized (God and Israel; Christ and the Church). The plain sense is erotic love poetry.

## Key translation issues

1. **Preserve the erotic plain sense.** The text is love poetry celebrating physical desire and beauty. Do not euphemize. Translations that consistently soften bodily language in the direction of spiritualized allegory are unfaithful to the author's text.
2. **דּוֹד** ("beloved / lover"): the male subject. "Beloved" is idiomatic; "lover" is also defensible. Pick one and be consistent across all occurrences in a given poem.
3. **רַעְיָה** ("my friend / my darling"): the male speaker's address to the woman. "Darling" is more intimate and carries the right affectional register; "my companion" undersells it.
4. **Geographical vocabulary** (Sharon, Lebanon, En-gedi, Tirzah, Damascus): keep all place names. Do not substitute generic landscape words. The specificity is part of the poetry's evocative geography.
5. **Botanical and zoological metaphors** (lilies, myrrh, henna, fawns, gazelles, foxes): render concretely and literally. The Hebrew imagery is precise — "foxes that ruin the vineyards" is a specific agricultural image, not a vague metaphor to be explained away.
6. **נֶפֶשׁ** (e.g., 1:7; 3:1–4): often "soul" in traditional renderings; here it functions as "I" or "my whole being." Context drives the choice; do not default to "soul" when "I" or "my heart" is more natural.

## Style / register

- High lyric register throughout. This is poetry; line structure, rhythm, and imagery are the substance of meaning.
- Preserve repetition — repeated refrains ("I adjure you, daughters of Jerusalem…"; "His left hand under my head…") are structural and deliberate.
- The female speaker has the majority of speech; her voice is assertive and independent — do not domesticate it.
- Preserve the imperative mood in invitations and commands ("draw me," "kiss me," "come away").

## Known pitfalls

- **Allegorizing in the translation itself.** The text supports allegorical reading, but the translation should render what is written, not what allegory makes of it.
- **Over-euphemizing body language.** The Hebrew uses concrete vocabulary for breasts, belly, thighs; render from the text.
- **Flattening the chorus/dialogue structure.** The three voices (female, male, chorus) should remain audibly distinct.
- **Rendering שִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים as "Song of Solomon."** "Song of Songs" preserves the superlative construction; "Solomon" is a late attribution and should not be in the main title.
