# 1 Enoch — book context for review and drafting

- **Work**: 1 Enoch / Ethiopic Enoch / *Mashafa Henok*
- **Form**: Composite apocalyptic work in 108 chapters, comprising five originally independent sections:
  1. **Book of the Watchers** (chs. 1–36) — fallen angels, cosmic tour, judgment
  2. **Book of the Parables / Similitudes** (chs. 37–71) — Son of Man, the Elect One, judgment of kings
  3. **Astronomical Book** (chs. 72–82) — calendar, sun and moon, astronomical cycles
  4. **Book of Dream Visions** (chs. 83–90) — Noah's flood vision, Animal Apocalypse
  5. **Epistle of Enoch** (chs. 91–108) — woes against sinners, exhortations, eschatological discourse, Apocalypse of Weeks
- **Language situation** (critical):
  - Our primary surviving *complete* witness is **Ge'ez (Classical Ethiopic)**.
  - **Greek** fragments survive for chs. 1–36 (Panopolitanus, Syncellus, Chester Beatty-Michigan Papyrus) and parts of 97–104, 106–107.
  - **Aramaic** Qumran fragments (4Q201–202, 204–212) survive for the Watchers, Astronomical Book, Dream Visions, and Epistle — but **NOT the Parables**, which are Ge'ez-only.
  - **Syriac**: a single citation.
- **Original language**: Semitic (Aramaic and/or Hebrew). The Ge'ez is a translation of a translation (Semitic → Greek → Ge'ez).
- **Date**: 3rd century BC (Astronomical Book, earliest layers of Watchers) through the 1st century AD (Parables are likely latest, 1st century AD). The Epistle sits in the 2nd–1st centuries BC.
- **Critical editions consulted**:
  - **R.H. Charles, *The Ethiopic Version of the Book of Enoch*, 1906** (Anecdota Oxoniensia): our primary Ge'ez scanned source.
  - **A. Dillmann, *Liber Henoch aethiopice*, 1851**: secondary Ge'ez witness for cross-checking.
  - Charles, *The Book of Enoch*, 1912 (English with apparatus): scholarly reference.
  - Nickelsburg & VanderKam, *1 Enoch* (Hermeneia commentary, 2004–2012): the modern standard, copyrighted.
  - Knibb, *The Ethiopic Book of Enoch*, 1978: another standard, copyrighted.
- **Original audience**: Second Temple Jews immersed in apocalyptic, priestly, and scribal wisdom traditions. The work shaped (and was canonical for) the Qumran community, early Christians (cited in Jude 14–15), and the Ethiopic Christian church (where it remains canonical to this day).

## What matters most for translation

1. **Treat the Ge'ez as the base text, but stay alert to where Greek or Aramaic evidence clarifies a difficult reading.** For chs. 1–36, 97–104, 106–107 the Greek may preserve readings closer to the original. For the Watchers, Astronomical Book, and parts of the Epistle, Qumran Aramaic fragments may resolve Ge'ez ambiguities. The Parables (37–71) have no Semitic/Greek check — extra caution there.

2. **Visionary and apocalyptic prose/poetry.** Do not flatten cosmic imagery into generic modern religious language. The text moves between:
   - Narrative third-person cosmic tour ("and I saw…")
   - First-person visionary report
   - Direct speech from God, angels, the Chosen One
   - Woes and blessings (formal liturgical form)
   Each register should read audibly distinct in English.

3. **Key technical vocabulary** — translate consistently:
   - **የኃሩ** / "the Chosen One" / "the Elect One" (ὁ ἐκλεκτός) — a specific messianic-eschatological figure in the Parables.
   - **ወልደ ሰብእ** / "Son of Man" (ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου) — in 1 En 46, 48, 62–63, 69–71. DO NOT harmonize with the NT Christological "Son of Man"; preserve Enoch's own usage, which has a distinct Second Temple trajectory.
   - **ጻድቃን** / "the righteous" and **ቅዱሳን** / "the holy ones" — often parallel but distinguishable; "holy ones" frequently = angels.
   - **ዐመፃ / ኃጢአት** / "iniquity / sin" — not interchangeable; track the underlying terms.
   - **ዕረፍት** / "rest" — eschatological reward, cf. Heb 4; preserve the specific Enochic usage.
   - **ርእስ ዘመን / ዕለታት** — "head/beginning of days" (1 En 46:1) cf. Dan 7:9.

4. **Do not over-harmonize with later Christian usage.** Terms like "Son of Man," "chosen ones," "righteous," "holy ones," "judgment," "throne," "enthronement" are part of a Second Temple Jewish idiom that the NT later inherits and transforms. Render from the local passage first, not from later doctrinal expectations.

5. **The Parables (chs. 37–71) demand extra restraint** because we have no Semitic or Greek check. Where the Ge'ez is uncertain, prefer transparent literal English plus a footnote over false confidence.

6. **Preserve repeated formulas.** Apocalyptic literature uses formulaic repetition (e.g., "And in those days…", "Blessed are…", "Woe to…"). Do not collapse repetitions for English "style."

7. **Where Jude 14–15 and other NT texts echo or cite Enoch**, preserve Enoch's *own* wording. Do not back-port the English of the canonical citation.

8. **Numerical and calendrical precision** in the Astronomical Book (72–82) is exact — the text presents a 364-day solar calendar in deliberate polemic against lunar observation. Don't smooth numerals for readability. A Ge'ez "one hundred and four and twenty" stays "one hundred and twenty-four," not "about a hundred."

## Style / register

- Aim for elevated but readable English.
- Preserve the force of visionary reports, angelic speech, woes, blessings, throne-scenes, and judgment scenes.
- Keep repeated apocalyptic formulas repeated.
- For the Astronomical Book, a slightly more restrained/technical register fits — it's didactic-scientific apocalyptic.
- For the Animal Apocalypse (chs. 85–90), preserve the zoomorphic code (white bulls, sheep, ravens, wolves) — do not gloss identifications inline; footnote instead.
- When the Ge'ez wording is uncertain, prefer transparent English plus a footnote over confident reconstruction.

## Known translation pitfalls

- **Ge'ez ሕልም** ("dream") vs **ራእይ** ("vision"): distinct words, distinct force. Not interchangeable.
- **መስፍን** ("prince"/"ruler") in Aramaic/Ge'ez contexts of fallen-angel leaders: often rendered "chief" or "prince" depending on context; cite Dillmann or Leslau.
- **ዘላለም / ለዓለም** ("forever"/"for eternity"): preserve the distinction between "eternal" and "everlasting" when the Ge'ez does.
- **OCR caveats**: our Ge'ez was OCRed with Gemini 3.1 Pro vision from the Charles 1906 scan. 2026-04-22 bake-off showed ~80% character accuracy at the best settings. Persistent diacritical errors are possible; consult the scanned witness directly when a verse is contested.
- **Verse numbering**: follows Charles 1906 / Charles 1912 English. Some chapters have non-standard verse counts because of manuscript variation; do not "correct" them to Nickelsburg's reconstruction unless the Ge'ez genuinely supports it.
- **The "Son of Man" passages (1 En 46, 48, 62, 69–71)** — avoid translating with any NT-inflected phrasing. "that Son of Man," "this Son of Man," "the Son of Man" are each separately attested and distinguishable in the Ge'ez.
