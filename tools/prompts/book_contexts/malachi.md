# Malachi — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for variants.
- **Form**: Disputational oracle — a series of divine assertions followed by an imagined objection from the people ("But you say…"), then a divine rebuttal. Six disputations. Four chapters (Hebrew: three in some LXX traditions).
- **Date**: Post-exilic, Persian period. Temple is rebuilt and functioning (1:10; 3:1), but priest corruption and popular disillusionment are advanced — likely 5th c. BC, possibly contemporary with Ezra-Nehemiah reforms.
- **Original audience**: Post-exilic Judean community disillusioned with the covenant because of economic hardship and perceived divine indifference.

## Key translation issues

1. **The disputational pattern**: The structural formula is crucial — "You say, X. But I say, Y." The Hebrew quotes the objectors precisely before rebutting. Render the dialogue structure clearly, including the ironic quotation of the people's words.
2. **מַלְאָכִי** ("my messenger / Malachi"): the name may be a pseudonym ("my messenger") rather than a proper name, given 3:1 ("I am sending my messenger"). Keep "Malachi" in the title but note the ambiguity.
3. **אָהַבְתִּי אֶתְכֶם** (1:2 — "I have loved you"): the opening divine assertion is perfect-tense — God's past and continuing love. The people's response ("How have you loved us?") introduces the disputational pattern.
4. **כֹּהֲנִים** ("priests") / **לֵוִיִּם** ("Levites"): the priestly critique (1:6–2:9) is central. Preserve the specific institutional vocabulary.
5. **גֵּרַשְׁתֶּם** / divorce controversy (2:16): one of the most contested verses. MT may read "For he hates sending away [= divorce]" (so ESV) or "he who hates, let him send away" (so RSV). The MT is genuinely ambiguous; render from the text and footnote the alternative.
6. **אֵלִיָּהוּ הַנָּבִיא** (4:5 — "Elijah the prophet"): eschatological promise cited in NT (Matthew 11:14; 17:10–12). Render as written; do not pre-interpret as John the Baptist.

## Style / register

- Dialogical, argumentative, direct. The oracle mode is assertive, even combative.
- Preserve the rhetorical question format (used extensively throughout).
- The closing eschatological promises (3:16–4:6) shift register to solemn promise.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening the disputational dialogue into monologue.** The back-and-forth objection-and-rebuttal pattern is the book's defining literary form.
- **Over-determining 2:16 in the direction of the divorce prohibition.** The text is contested; render what the Hebrew says, note the difficulty.
