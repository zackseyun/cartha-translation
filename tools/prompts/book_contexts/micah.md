# Micah — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 7 chapters.
- **Form**: Prophetic oracles — judgment and restoration alternating throughout. From a small-town Judean prophet (Moresheth).
- **Date**: ca. 735–700 BC. Contemporary with Isaiah in the south and Hosea in the north.
- **Original audience**: Judah under Assyrian threat; critique of corrupt leadership, false prophets, economic oppression.

## Key translation issues

1. **Micah 6:8**: "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness (חֶסֶד), and to walk humbly with your God?" One of the most quoted OT verses. Key terms: מִשְׁפָּט = "justice," חֶסֶד = "kindness" or "steadfast love," צְנֵעַ לֶכֶת = "walk humbly." Keep "humbly" — not "carefully."
2. **Micah 5:2** ("But you, O Bethlehem…"): "From you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel." Render the Hebrew as it stands — a future leader from Bethlehem. Matthew cites it in 2:6; do not pre-interpret in the OT text.
3. **"Beat swords into plowshares"** (4:3 // Isaiah 2:4): The famous peace oracle. "Swords into plowshares and spears into pruning hooks." Keep the exact wording — these agricultural implements are specific. Note the parallel in Joel 3:10 (reversed).
4. **The lawsuit oracle** (6:1–8): "Hear what the LORD says: Rise, plead your case before the mountains…" — a covenant lawsuit (rib). "Plead your case" or "present your case" — the legal metaphor must be preserved.
5. **False prophets** (3:5–8): "Who cry 'Peace' when they have something to eat, but declare war against those who put nothing in their mouths." Keep the irony.
6. **Micah's personal confession** (7:7–20): Shifts to first person — "But as for me, I will look to the LORD." The personal confession followed by confidence is a distinct genre; preserve the speaker shift.

## Style / register

- Alternating between fierce judgment and tender restoration.
- The corruption accusations (ch. 3) are concrete and blunt.
- The closing doxology (7:18–20) is hymnic — render with elevated register.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying "humbly" in 6:8** — the word is specific and traditional.
- **Pre-reading 5:2 through the NT** in the translation text itself.
