# Ruth — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for text-critical notes.
- **Form**: Short narrative prose. Four chapters, tight unit. Framed as a story set "in the days when the judges ruled."
- **Date**: Widely debated. Post-exilic setting is most common scholarly consensus (late Persian, 5th–4th c. BC), though some argue for pre-exilic composition. The audience is Jewish, and the book makes a pointed theological argument about loyalty and providence.
- **Original audience**: Israelite/Jewish readers for whom חֶסֶד (loyal love, covenant faithfulness) is a theological category, and for whom the genealogy at the close (4:17–22) signals a Davidic connection.

## Key translation issues

1. **חֶסֶד** — the single most important word in the book. Appears at 1:8, 2:20, 3:10. "Kindness" undersells it; "steadfast love" (NRSV) is better; "loyalty" is defensible. The word names behavior that exceeds contractual obligation and reflects covenant commitment. Flag each occurrence and note the semantic range; do not default to "kindness" without a note.
2. **גֹּאֵל / גָּאַל** ("kinsman-redeemer / to redeem"): a legal-institutional term. The redeemer is the next-of-kin obligated to buy back land, marry a dead brother's widow (levirate context), and protect family honor. Render as "kinsman-redeemer" or "redeemer" consistently, not "close relative" alone — the legal force must be audible.
3. **רוּת** ("Ruth"): the name is preserved, not translated. Moabite identity matters — the text emphasizes it deliberately (1:4, 1:22, 2:2, etc.).
4. **עָלָה עַל-כְּנָפֶךָ** (3:9 — "spread your wings over me"): covenant-protection idiom echoing Boaz's earlier blessing (2:12). Render literally; the echo is intentional.
5. **יָשַׁב בַּשַּׁעַר** ("sitting at the gate"): the legal/civic space for binding transactions. Keep "gate" — do not modernize to "public square" or "courthouse."

## Style / register

- Narrative prose with dialogue. The dialogue between Naomi, Ruth, and Boaz has a courtly formal register at times (3:9–13); render that formal tone.
- Ruth's famous speech (1:16–17) is elevated and should read as a solemn personal commitment, not casual conversation.
- The genealogy (4:18–22) is list-form; keep its rhythmic list structure.
- Do not over-sentimentalize the loyalty theme — the text presents it with restraint.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening חֶסֶד to "kindness."** The word names the theological core of the book; "kindness" does not carry the covenant freight.
- **Translating גֹּאֵל as merely "relative."** The legal obligation is the point.
- **Missing the double meaning of "under your wings" at 3:9** as echo of 2:12. The English should preserve the same image word in both places.
