# Ezra — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). Contains extended Aramaic sections (4:8–6:18; 7:12–26). LXX (1 Esdras, Esdras B/Rahlfs) for text-critical reference.
- **Form**: Narrative history with embedded documents (decrees, letters, lists). Often treated as a single composition with Nehemiah (Ezra-Nehemiah). Contains first-person memoir sections attributed to Ezra himself (7:27–9:15).
- **Date**: Final form likely late 5th–early 4th c. BC. The book covers the return from Babylonian exile under Cyrus (538 BC) through Ezra's reform mission (ca. 458 BC).
- **Original audience**: Post-exilic Jewish community in Yehud (Persian province of Judah), wrestling with identity, covenant renewal, and the status of mixed marriages.

## Key translation issues

1. **Aramaic sections** (4:8–6:18; 7:12–26): The shift in language is linguistically significant. The Aramaic consists largely of official Persian royal documents (Aramaic was the imperial language). Render with a slightly more official/administrative register than the surrounding Hebrew narrative.
2. **תּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה** ("the Torah of Moses"): always render as "the Law of Moses" or "the Torah of Moses" — not "the teaching" or "the instruction," which undersells the institutional weight the book places on it.
3. **גּוֹלָה / בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה** ("exiles / the children of the exile"): the technical term for the community of returned exiles who define themselves as the legitimate continuing Israel. Render consistently as "exiles" or "the exilic community," not simply "the people."
4. **הִתְקַדֵּשׁ / טָהֵר** ("to consecrate oneself / to purify"): ritual purity language; keep the cultic register.
5. **נִשִּׂים נָכְרִיּוֹת** ("foreign wives"): the controversy over intermarriage (chs. 9–10). Render literally "foreign wives" — the foreignness (נָכְרִיּוֹת specifically) is the point, not mere outsider status.

## Style / register

- Historical narrative alternating with lists, prayers, and official documents.
- Ezra's prayer (ch. 9) is elevated penitential prose; preserve its confessional register.
- The lists (returning exiles, priests, Levites) are meant to establish legitimacy — render them accurately without smoothing variant spellings.
- The Persian royal decrees (Cyrus's edict, letters in chs. 4–7) have an official administrative style; render with formal register.

## Known pitfalls

- **Smoothing the Aramaic/Hebrew language switch.** The shift is deliberate and historically motivated; both sections should be clearly rendered from their respective source languages.
- **Modernizing the purity/holiness language.** Keep the cultic register; do not soften "consecrate" or "defile."
- **Under-translating גּוֹלָה.** This group-identity term carries the entire theology of return and restoration.
