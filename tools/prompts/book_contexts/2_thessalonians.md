# 2 Thessalonians — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 3 chapters.
- **Form**: Pauline letter (authorship debated). Corrects a misunderstanding about the timing of Christ's return and encourages steadfastness.
- **Date**: ca. 51 AD (if Pauline) or ca. 80–100 AD (if pseudonymous).
- **Original audience**: Thessalonian Christians who believe the Day of the Lord has already arrived.

## Key translation issues

1. **ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἀνομίας** (2:3 — "the man of lawlessness / the man of sin"): The eschatological antagonist. Keep "the man of lawlessness" (NA28 reads ἀνομίας; some MSS read ἁμαρτίας, "sin"). Do not substitute "the Antichrist" — that title is from 1 John, not here.
2. **ὁ κατέχων / τὸ κατέχον** (2:6–7 — "the one who restrains / what restrains"): One of the most debated passages in the NT. Render literally: "the one who restrains" (masculine, 2:7) and "what restrains" (neuter, 2:6). Do not identify "the restrainer" in the text.
3. **ἀποστασία** (2:3 — "apostasy / rebellion / falling away"): "The rebellion" (NRSV) or "the apostasy" (ESV) — "falling away" (KJV) is vague. "Rebellion" or "apostasy" are more precise.
4. **παρουσία** (2:1, 8, 9): Same eschatological term as 1 Thessalonians. Keep "coming" consistently.
5. **"Disorderly" (ἄτακτος, 3:6–12)**: Originally a military term for soldiers out of formation; here applied to those who refuse to work. "Idle" (NIV) or "living in idleness" (NRSV) — "disorderly" (NASB) may be closer to the sense. Footnote.

## Style / register

- More formal and distant than 1 Thessalonians.
- The eschatological section (2:1–12) is dense and requires precise rendering.

## Known pitfalls

- **Identifying "the man of lawlessness" in the text** — render the term, leave identification to footnotes.
- **Resolving the restrainer's identity** — translate literally, footnote the debate.
