# 3 Maccabees — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs). Composed in Greek; not actually about the Maccabean revolt. Seven chapters.
- **Form**: Novella / diaspora tale. Set in Egypt under Ptolemy IV Philopator (221–204 BC). The king attempts to force Egyptian Jews into the cult of Dionysus, then tries to kill them with elephants; God intervenes and saves them.
- **Date**: ca. 1st c. BC–1st c. AD. Alexandria.
- **Original audience**: Egyptian Diaspora Jews, celebrating divine deliverance of their community.

## Key translation issues

1. **The rhetoric of persecution and deliverance**: The book dramatizes Jewish suffering and divine rescue. The dramatic reversals (persecution → prayer → miraculous rescue → celebration) are the structural spine. Preserve the dramatic tension.
2. **Prayer sections** (3:1–7; 6:1–15): Eleazar's prayer (6:1–15) is the theological center. It is an extended appeal to divine precedents of deliverance (angels at Sennacherib's camp, the three young men in the furnace, Daniel in the lion's den). Render with elevated liturgical-prayer register.
3. **Elephants**: the threat of death by elephant is vivid and specific; render literally.
4. **Royal decrees**: the king issues two contradictory decrees (permitting, then reversing the persecution). Official register required.
5. **ἐπιφάνεια** ("appearing / divine manifestation"): God's visible intervention in 6:18. "Divine manifestation" or "epiphany" — a technical term for divine intervention.
6. **The feast of deliverance** (closing chapters): a seven-day feast is established as a memorial. The institution narrative should echo the Purim/Passover institution pattern.

## Style / register

- Hellenistic novella; more theatrical and dramatic than 1–2 Maccabees.
- The author uses elaborate sentence structures and heightened rhetoric.
- Do not flatten into plain narrative — the stylistic elaborateness is part of the point.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening the prayer rhetoric.** The prayers are highly formal and literary.
- **Losing the dramatic irony** in the king's two contradictory decrees.
