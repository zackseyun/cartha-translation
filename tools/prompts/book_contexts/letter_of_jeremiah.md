# Letter of Jeremiah — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Ziegler). One chapter (73 verses). Treated as a separate book from Baruch in this corpus (appears as Baruch 6 in some traditions).
- **Form**: Polemical letter addressed to Jews about to go into Babylonian exile, warning them against idolatry. A refrain ("they are not gods, so do not fear them") recurs throughout as a structural anchor.
- **Date**: Uncertain. Possibly 3rd–2nd c. BC. Attribution to Jeremiah is literary; the actual historical context of the letter is the hellenistic diaspora.
- **Original audience**: Jewish diaspora facing the temptation of assimilation into Hellenistic idol culture.

## Key translation issues

1. **The refrain** (vv. 14–15, 22–23, 28–29, 64–65, 68–69 and variants): "Therefore they are not gods; do not be afraid of them." This refrain is the structural and theological spine of the letter. It must be rendered consistently with the same wording every time it appears — it is a liturgical-rhetorical device. Do not vary the English for stylistic diversity.
2. **The idol descriptions**: The letter catalogs all the ways idols are helpless (they cannot speak, see, save themselves from rust, etc.). The catalog is deliberate and repetitive — preserve the cumulative rhetorical force. Do not condense.
3. **θεοί / εἴδωλα** ("gods / idols"): the letter denies that idols are θεοί. Keep the distinction between these words.
4. **The comparison with a scarecrow** (v. 69): "They are like a scarecrow in a cucumber field." Keep the agricultural metaphor precisely.

## Style / register

- Satirical and polemical throughout. The tone is dismissive of idols and earnest in warning.
- The cumulative listing technique is deliberately repetitive — the repetition is the point.
- The letter is more a sermon/tract than a real letter; the register is didactic-polemical.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying the refrain for English style.** Do not. The repeated exact wording is the form.
- **Condensing the idol catalog.** The repetitiveness is the argument, not a stylistic defect.
