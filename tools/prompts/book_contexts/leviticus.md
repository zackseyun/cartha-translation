# Leviticus — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 27 chapters.
- **Form**: Priestly law code. Sacrifice regulations (1–7), priestly ordination (8–10), purity laws (11–15), Day of Atonement (16), Holiness Code (17–26), appendix (27).
- **Date**: Final priestly editing likely post-exilic (6th–5th cent. BC), though core traditions are older.
- **Original audience**: Israel's priestly and cultic practice instructions.

## Key translation issues

1. **Cultic vocabulary must be stable throughout**: עֹלָה (burnt offering), מִנְחָה (grain/cereal offering), שְׁלָמִים (peace/fellowship offerings), חַטָּאת (sin offering), אָשָׁם (guilt/reparation offering). Establish these terms in chapters 1–7 and never vary them.
2. **כַּפָּרָה / כִּפֶּר** ("atonement / to make atonement"): The cultic core of Leviticus. The root כפר appears throughout. Always "atonement" — not "covering" or "expiation" (though either is theologically defensible, consistency matters more than the debate).
3. **קָדוֹשׁ / קְדֻשָּׁה** ("holy / holiness"): "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy" (19:2). This is the Holiness Code's motto. Keep "holy" and "holiness" — not "pure" or "set apart" as primary renderings.
4. **The Day of Atonement** (ch. 16): עֲזָאזֵל (16:8, 10, 26 — "Azazel / scapegoat"): The word may be a proper name (a desert demon) or a description ("scapegoat"). Keep the transliteration "Azazel" or "the scapegoat" — footnote the debate.
5. **Repeated formulas**: "The LORD spoke to Moses, saying…" opens nearly every section. Keep it exactly — do not vary.
6. **טָמֵא / טָהוֹר** ("unclean / clean"): The purity binary is pervasive. Always "unclean" and "clean" — not "impure/pure" (though equivalent, consistency matters for the code's internal logic).
7. **The Holiness Code** (17–26): Direct second-person commands — "You shall" and "You shall not." Keep the direct address.

## Style / register

- Legal, formulaic, repetitive. The repetition is the form — do not edit it out.
- Some passages (e.g., the Day of Atonement, 16; and the blessings/curses, 26) have elevated, solemn register.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying the offering names** for style — consistency matters far more than elegance here.
- **Softening "unclean"** to "impure" — "unclean" is the established cultic term.
- **Breaking the formula structure** of "The LORD spoke to Moses, saying…"
