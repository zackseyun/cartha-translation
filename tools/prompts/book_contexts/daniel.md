# Daniel — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 12 chapters. Note: the book is bilingual — Hebrew (1:1–2:4a; 8–12) and Aramaic (2:4b–7:28).
- **Form**: Court tales (1–6) and apocalyptic visions (7–12). The two sections are structurally and linguistically distinct.
- **Date**: Final form ca. 165 BC (Antiochene crisis under Antiochus IV Epiphanes), though the court tales may be older.
- **Original audience**: Jews under Seleucid persecution — encouragement to remain faithful and assurance of divine vindication.

## Key translation issues

1. **The bilingual structure**: Mark the language transition at 2:4b — the text shifts into Aramaic. The Aramaic section (2:4b–7:28) should not be translated as if it were Hebrew; Aramaic has different idioms and vocabulary.
2. **בַּר אֱנָשׁ / כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ** (7:13 — "one like a son of man / one like a human being"): The Aramaic phrase is כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ. "One like a son of man" is the traditional rendering; "one like a human being" is the literal Aramaic. The phrase is central to NT Christology (it is Jesus's self-designation). Render literally: "one like a son of man" — footnote the NT use but do not import it into the translation.
3. **The four kingdoms and the statue** (ch. 2) and **four beasts** (ch. 7): Render symbolic imagery without decoding it in the text. "A great statue" — not "a symbol of world empires." Translate the image; footnote the interpretation.
4. **The "Ancient of Days"** (עַתִּיק יוֹמַיָּא, 7:9, 13, 22): Keep "the Ancient of Days" — it is the established rendering of the divine title.
5. **קֵץ** ("end / appointed time"): Used extensively in chs. 8–12 for the "end" of the appointed period. Render consistently as "end" or "appointed time" — not "last days" (imports eschatological assumptions).
6. **מִכָאֵל / גַּבְרִיאֵל**: Angel names — keep transliterated: "Michael," "Gabriel."
7. **Numbers in the visions**: 1,260, 1,290, 1,335 days; "a time, times, and half a time" (7:25; 12:7). Render exactly — do not explain in the text.

## Style / register

- Court tales (1–6): narrative prose, sometimes with a storytelling charm (the three friends in the furnace, the writing on the wall).
- Visions (7–12): intense apocalyptic imagery. The language is deliberately symbolic and strange.
- The transition between registers (tale vs. vision) should be audible.

## Known pitfalls

- **Decoding symbolic imagery in the text itself.** Translate the symbols; leave interpretation to footnotes.
- **Harmonizing "son of man" with the NT.** Translate Daniel on its own terms.
- **Ignoring the Aramaic-Hebrew bilingualism.** The sections were composed in different languages for literary reasons.
