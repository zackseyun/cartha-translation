# Book context — Didache (Διδαχὴ τῶν δώδεκα ἀποστόλων)

## Author and audience

Anonymous, plural ("we" voice in places). The text presents itself as
the **teaching of the twelve apostles** to the gentile churches —
likely a community handbook composed in Syria or Palestine, late 1st
to early 2nd century CE. The intended audience is **catechumens and
new gentile converts** who need a practical compendium of moral
instruction (Two Ways), liturgical practice (baptism, fasting,
Eucharist, prayer), and church order (handling visiting prophets and
apostles, the role of bishops and deacons, eschatological vigilance).

The register is **catechetical and prescriptive** — short imperatives,
contrasting commands ("do not / but rather"), formulaic prayers, and
plain didactic prose. Not literary Greek; the syntax is direct and
parallels the LXX more than classical authors.

## Source language

**Greek.** The single near-complete witness is **Codex
Hierosolymitanus H54 (1056 CE)**, discovered and published by
Bryennios in 1873. Earlier and partial witnesses include the Coptic
fragment, the Georgian, the Apostolic Constitutions VII (which
incorporates a paraphrased Didache), and Latin extracts in the
*Doctrina Apostolorum*.

Our primary source: **Bryennios's Greek text of H54.** Where H54 is
clearly corrupt, the Apostolic Constitutions and the Latin tradition
are consulted as secondary witnesses.

## What the original audience would hear

- **The Two Ways (chs 1–6)** is a Jewish moral instruction
  framework, christianized at the front (1:3-2:1 — gospel-flavored
  expansion). Audience would recognize the Two Ways pattern from
  Jewish ethical literature (cf. Barnabas 18-20, 1QS, Testaments).
  The English should preserve the contrastive parallelism, not
  smooth it out.
- **Liturgical material (chs 7-10, 14)** — baptism and Eucharist
  formulas. The audience would memorize and recite these. Cadence
  matters. Greek participial constructions in prayers carry
  invocational weight that flat English imperatives drop.
- **Church-order material (chs 11-13, 15)** — practical, almost
  procedural. The audience would use this as a manual when an
  itinerant prophet showed up. Specific vocabulary about prophets,
  apostles, bishops, deacons must preserve the early-Christian
  technical sense, not import later ecclesiastical English.
- **Eschatological close (ch 16)** — apocalyptic, urgent. The
  audience would feel the pressure of imminent expectation. English
  that softens this into general moral exhortation loses the author's
  intent.

## Translation challenges specific to this book

1. **Δίδαχη** itself: "teaching" is correct but flat. The author
   means an authoritative apostolic instruction, not a casual lesson.
2. **Ὁδός** (Way) vs **τρόπος** (manner): the Two Ways are
   ontological paths, not lifestyle preferences.
3. **Ἀπόστολος** and **προφήτης** in chs 11-13: technical roles in
   the early itinerant ministry, not abstract titles. Specifics about
   how long a prophet may stay (2 days = welcome; 3 days = false
   prophet) make sense only if the audience grasped these as concrete
   roles.
4. **Εὐχαριστία** (chs 9-10): both "thanksgiving" and a developing
   technical term for the Lord's Supper. The audience was at the
   transition. Translating one way or the other forces a clarity the
   original lacked.
5. **Marana tha** (10:6): the only Aramaic phrase. Preserving the
   transliteration honors what the author intentionally left
   untranslated.

## What NOT to import

- Later catholic / orthodox sacramental theology
- Reformation-era debates over baptism mode or eucharistic presence
- Modern individualistic devotional language

The author is talking to a 1st-2nd century gentile community living
in expectation of Christ's return, organizing themselves around
apostolic teaching. Render what they would have heard.
