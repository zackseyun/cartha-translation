# Esther — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 10 chapters. NOTE: The COB follows the MT. The LXX/Greek Esther has six major additions (Additions A–F) not present in the MT; these are in the Deuterocanon as separate additions (see additions_to_esther.md for those).
- **Form**: Court narrative — a diaspora tale set in the Persian court of Ahasuerus (Xerxes). The only canonical OT book that never mentions God.
- **Date**: Probably 4th–3rd cent. BC. Set in the Persian period.
- **Original audience**: Diaspora Jews — encouragement that Jewish identity can survive and prevail even in exile.

## Key translation issues

1. **God's absence**: The MT Esther never mentions God. Do not insert "God" or "the LORD" where the Hebrew does not have it. The absence is intentional.
2. **Proper names**: Ahasuerus (= Xerxes), Mordecai, Esther (also Hadassah), Haman, Vashti, Bigthana/Bigthan. Keep consistent spellings throughout.
3. **The reversal structure**: The entire plot is built on reversals — what is prepared for others is turned against the preparer. The vocabulary of reversal (הֲפַךְ — "turn/reverse") should be audible where possible.
4. **"For such a time as this"** (4:14 — לְעֵת כָּזֹאת): Mordecai's challenge to Esther. "Who knows whether you have not come to the kingdom for such a time as this?" Keep "for such a time as this" — it is the rhetorical climax.
5. **The feast vocabulary**: Purim (פּוּר = lot) and the two-day feast celebration in ch. 9. The etymological connection between Purim and "lot" should be preserved in the narrative explanation.
6. **Haman's "gallows"** (עֵץ — "gallows / stake / tree / pole"): The Hebrew is simply עֵץ = "wood/tree/pole." The fifty-cubit structure (about 75 feet high) may be better rendered "stake" or "pole" than "gallows" (which implies a hanging frame). Footnote.

## Style / register

- Light, ironic, comic in places — a literary masterpiece of diaspora fiction.
- The pace is quick and the dialogue is sharp.
- The reversal scenes have a farcical quality (Haman forced to honor Mordecai, 6:6–11).

## Known pitfalls

- **Inserting "God" or divine providence language** where the MT has none.
- **Missing the irony and comedy** of scenes like Haman's self-incriminating suggestion (6:6–11).
