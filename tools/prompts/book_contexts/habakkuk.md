# Habakkuk — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs), Qumran pesher 1QpHab, and Habakkuk DSS fragments for text-critical reference.
- **Form**: Prophetic dialogue (prophet questions God, God answers) plus a psalm (ch. 3). Three chapters.
- **Date**: Pre-exilic, Babylonian period. The Chaldeans (Babylonians) are a present or imminent threat (1:6), placing composition ca. 610–600 BC.
- **Original audience**: Judah facing Babylonian aggression. The prophet wrestles with why God would use a wicked nation to punish his people.

## Key translation issues

1. **צַדִּיק בֶּאֱמוּנָתוֹ יִחְיֶה** (2:4 — "the righteous shall live by his faithfulness/faith"): one of the most cited verses in the NT (Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11, Hebrews 10:38) and in the Qumran Pesher. The Hebrew אֱמוּנָה means "faithfulness, reliability, steadfastness" — not primarily intellectual assent. The Qumran community read it as faithfulness to Torah; Paul reads it through a Christological lens. The COB translation should render the Hebrew precisely — "faithfulness" is more accurate than "faith" for the Hebrew sense. Footnote the NT usage separately.
2. **חָמָס** ("violence"): a recurring term (1:2, 3, 9; 2:8, 17). Specific word for injustice and brutality — render consistently as "violence," not "wrong" or "harm."
3. **עָמַל** ("trouble/affliction/labor"): paired with חָמָס in 1:3. "Trouble" or "affliction" — not the weak "distress."
4. **הַתִּכּוֹנַנְתִּי עַל-מִשְׁמֶרֶת** (2:1 — "I will stand at my watchpost"): the prophet as watchman waiting for God's answer. Keep the military-guard imagery.
5. **Chapter 3 (the Psalm of Habakkuk)**: Contains archaic Hebrew poetry with multiple hapax legomena and textual difficulties. The Selah markers (3:3, 9, 13) indicate liturgical use. Render the archaic theophanic imagery (God striding out from Teman, shaking mountains) literally and powerfully — do not modernize the cosmic imagery.

## Style / register

- The dialogue sections (1:2–2:4) read as urgent prayer/complaint followed by divine oracle. Preserve the lament register.
- The woe oracles (2:6–20) are formal: "Woe to him who…" Keep the formulaic "Woe" — do not soften to "How terrible for…"
- Chapter 3 is elevated archaic poetry (marked as "a prayer of Habakkuk the prophet, according to Shigionoth" — keep this superscription). The theophanic imagery is deliberately overwhelming.

## Known pitfalls

- **Translating אֱמוּנָה as "faith"** (importing the NT sense back into the OT verse). Render as "faithfulness" with a footnote to the NT usage.
- **Flattening the theophanic imagery in ch. 3.** The cosmic battle imagery is the point — do not tone it down.
- **Missing the structural tension of the dialogue.** The book is deliberately unresolved — Habakkuk's final response (3:17–19) answers the theological question not by explanation but by resolve. Preserve that.
