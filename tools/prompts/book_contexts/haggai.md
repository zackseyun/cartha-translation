# Haggai — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for text-critical notes.
- **Form**: Prophetic speeches with dated formulaic superscriptions. Two chapters, four oracles, all precisely dated to 520 BC (second year of Darius I).
- **Date**: 520 BC (within the book itself). Post-exilic, during the interruption of Temple rebuilding under Persian rule.
- **Original audience**: The returned exilic community in Jerusalem and their governor Zerubbabel and high priest Joshua, who have halted Temple reconstruction.

## Key translation issues

1. **הָבֵא / שִׂימוּ לְבַבְכֶם** ("bring / set your heart / consider"): a repeated call to self-examination. Render the Hebrew idiom accurately: "Consider your ways" (literally "set your hearts on your ways") — not the looser "think about it."
2. **הַבַּיִת הַזֶּה** ("this house"): the Temple. Repeated throughout. Keep "this house" — not "the temple" — when the Hebrew uses הַבַּיִת. The deicitic "this" has rhetorical force.
3. **כָּבוֹד** (2:3, 7, 9 — "glory"): the glory of the former Temple (Solomon's) vs. the coming glory of the restored Temple. Keep "glory" consistently — do not vary to "splendor" or "majesty" within this book.
4. **הֶחֱמַדְתִּי אֶת-כָּל-הַגּוֹיִם** (2:7 — "the desire/treasures of all nations"): a contested phrase. Hebrew חֶמְדַּת הַגּוֹיִם ("the desirable things of the nations" — plural in MT) has been read messianically ("the desired one of all nations") especially in Vulgate (*desideratus cunctis gentibus*). The MT plural makes a material referent more likely. Render from the Hebrew; footnote the messianic reading tradition.
5. **Covenant formula** (2:4–5): "I am with you" + "My Spirit remains in your midst" — covenantal reassurance language. Preserve the first-person divine speech with its covenantal weight.

## Style / register

- Short, dated, official-prophetic prose with oracular speeches embedded.
- The dated superscriptions mark the text's historical anchoring — render them precisely.
- The rhetorical questions (1:4; 2:3, 12–13) are key structural moves; preserve them as questions.

## Known pitfalls

- **Harmonizing 2:7 to the Vulgate/messianic reading** without noting the MT difference.
- **Losing the precision of the date formulae.** These anchor the book historically; translate them exactly.
