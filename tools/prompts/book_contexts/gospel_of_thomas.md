# Book context — Gospel of Thomas (ⲡⲉⲩⲁⲅⲅⲉⲗⲓⲟⲛ ⲡⲕⲁⲧⲁ ⲑⲱⲙⲁⲥ)

## Form and authorship

A **sayings collection** of 114 logia (numbered units) attributed
to "the living Jesus." Anonymous; the framing prologue invokes
"Didymos Judas Thomas" as the recorder, but this is a literary
device, not authorial signature.

**Date.** The community of the surviving Coptic redaction is **4th
century**, but the underlying Greek tradition is much earlier —
the **Oxyrhynchus papyri (P.Oxy. 1, 654, 655)** preserve fragments
of a Greek version dated to the early-to-mid **2nd century CE**, and
some sayings show overlap with Q-tradition material that may push
parts back to the 1st century.

**Audience.** The text was used in **early Christian / Jewish-
Christian wisdom-tradition communities**, most likely in **Syria** or
**Egypt**. The intended audience is **insiders** — people already
familiar with Jesus-tradition who are being invited to read his
sayings as **secret wisdom requiring inward decipherment**
("whoever finds the interpretation of these sayings will not taste
death" — saying 1).

## Source languages

The translation should be reviewed against **two source layers**:

1. **Coptic primary** — Nag Hammadi Codex II.2 (NHC II.2), copied
   c. 350 CE, written in **Sahidic Coptic**. This is the only
   complete witness. Lexicon: Crum, *A Coptic Dictionary*.
2. **Greek fragmentary** — P.Oxy. 1, 654, 655, dated to ~200 CE.
   These cover portions of the prologue, sayings 1-7, 24, 26-33, 36
   (and a few others). Where Greek survives, it is **closer to the
   urtext** than the Coptic; where Coptic differs from Greek, the
   Coptic represents a later (sometimes theologically shifted)
   redaction. The lexicon for the Greek fragments is BDAG.

The translator should know **which witness the verse rests on**.
Where both exist, the review should consider whether our English
favors the right witness (and footnote the variant).

## Register and voice

- **Aphoristic and gnomic.** Most logia are short, declarative,
  often paradoxical. The English must preserve **brevity and
  punch**. Do not pad with explanatory connectives.
- **Riddle-like / parable form** in some logia (e.g., 8 fisherman
  parable, 9 sower, 21 shepherd-children, 64 great-banquet, 96
  woman-with-yeast, 97 woman-with-meal, 109 hidden-treasure). These
  parallel canonical synoptic parables but **always with notable
  divergences in detail or punchline** — the English must preserve
  the divergences exactly, not assimilate to the canonical wording.
- **Wisdom-tradition register** (proverbs, beatitudes, woes,
  exhortations to find / understand / be alone). Not narrative.
- **First-person Jesus sayings** with no narrative frame. The
  audience is invited into direct address.

## What the original audience would hear

- A collection of Jesus's **inner teachings**, not the public
  proclamation of the canonical gospels. The framing positions these
  as **esoteric**: meant for those with ears to hear.
- A theological orientation that emphasizes **inward
  transformation**, **unity** (becoming "one" / "single"
  μοναχός / ⲟⲩⲁ), **finding the kingdom within and around** rather
  than awaiting an apocalyptic future kingdom. Not the
  apocalyptic-eschatological frame of Mark 13.
- **Sayings that read as familiar from Matthew, Mark, Luke** but
  with subtle shifts. Audience steeped in oral Jesus-tradition would
  catch the divergences and feel the text positioning itself
  alongside (not against) the gospels.
- **Sayings that have no canonical parallel** (e.g., 7
  lion-eating-man, 22 children-becoming-one, 50 light-questions,
  77 pantheistic-Christ). These distinct logia carry the text's
  most distinctive theology and must NOT be flattened into
  canonical-Gospel English vocabulary.

## Translation challenges specific to this text

1. **Coptic ⲙⲟⲛⲁⲭⲟⲥ / "single one" / "solitary"** (logia 16, 49,
   75). Loanword from Greek. Translation choice — "single,"
   "solitary," "monk" — has theological weight. Original audience
   would hear "the unified one who has integrated inner duality,"
   not the later monastic technical sense.
2. **ⲙⲛ̄ⲧⲉⲣⲟ / βασιλεία / "kingdom"** — this text uses "kingdom"
   constantly but means something **non-apocalyptic**: a present
   inner-and-outer reality the wise can perceive. English "kingdom
   of God" carries Synoptic weight that distorts. Consider whether
   our English preserves the Thomas distinctness.
3. **ⲟⲩⲁ ⲟⲩⲱⲧ / "one" / "single"** — repeatedly. The author's
   theology of unity. Render consistently, not paraphrased away.
4. **Parable wording divergent from canonical.** When Thomas's
   parable says "X" and Matthew's parallel says "Y," the English
   must say X, not Y. Reviewers should check parables against the
   Coptic carefully.
5. **The "living Jesus"** (prologue, saying 52). Not the resurrected
   Christ of Pauline theology, not the historical Jesus of the
   Synoptics — Thomas's own theological category. The English
   "living" carries the right ambiguity; don't resolve it.
6. **Greek loanwords in Coptic** (ψυχή, σάρξ, μοναχός, κόσμος,
   etc.). Coptic preserves them transliterated. Reviewers should
   know which Greek term underlies a Coptic loan and whether the
   English faithfully renders the underlying Greek sense.
7. **Saying 114** (controversial — "make Mary male"). Whatever the
   English chooses here, it must not soften or paraphrase what the
   author actually wrote. Footnote the interpretive history; do not
   sanitize the text.

## Theological framing for the reviewer

Thomas is **not Gnostic in the demiurgic sense** (no evil creator,
no Yaldabaoth, no full Sethian or Valentinian cosmology). It IS
**inward / mystical / wisdom-oriented** in a way that diverges from
canonical synoptic-apocalyptic. Treat it as a distinct early
Christian wisdom tradition and **render what it says, not what
canonical Christianity later wished it had said**.

## What NOT to import

- Synoptic Gospel English vocabulary where Thomas uses different
  Coptic / Greek (do not "fix" Thomas to match Matthew).
- Later Gnostic systematizing (do not insert demiurge or pleroma
  language that Thomas does not contain).
- Apologetic flattening that removes Thomas's distinctive emphases
  ("kingdom is within you" must remain; do not retreat to safer
  canonical formulas).
- Modern liberal Jesus-Seminar readings that strip the text of all
  esoteric register.

The author is an early Christian wisdom-collector preserving sayings
of "the living Jesus" for a community of insider-readers in 2nd
century Egypt or Syria. Render what *that* community would have
heard.
