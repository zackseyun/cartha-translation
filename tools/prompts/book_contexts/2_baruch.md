# 2 Baruch — book context for review and drafting

- **Work**: 2 Baruch / Syriac Apocalypse of Baruch
- **Form**: Jewish apocalypse with a closing epistle
- **Likely date**: late 1st to early 2nd century CE, in the aftermath of the destruction of the Second Temple
- **Primary surviving witness**: Syriac
- **Secondary witnesses**: fragmentary Greek and Latin traces; Kmosko 1907 as a secondary Syriac / Latin scholarly control witness
- **Likely original audience**: Jews wrestling with the meaning of Jerusalem’s fall, covenant judgment, restoration, and the fate of the righteous
- **Translation posture**: keep Ceriani 1871 Syriac primary; use Kmosko, Charles 1896, and wider control material to clarify difficult or unstable chapter edges, not to override the base witness

## What matters most for review

1. **This is apocalyptic consolation after catastrophe.** Preserve lament, protest, and judgment rhetoric; do not flatten it into generic devotional language.
2. **Baruch’s voice is not neutral.** He grieves, protests, asks hard questions, and receives visionary explanations. The English should preserve that emotional and rhetorical movement.
3. **The book has a real structural turn from apocalypse to epistle.** The closing epistle should still sound like admonitory Baruch material, but reviewers should preserve the shift in mode and audience.
4. **Do not import 4 Ezra phrasing just because the books are adjacent in theme.** Similar theology does not mean identical wording.
5. **Where the source bucket still depends on medium-confidence chapter edges, prefer honest notes and careful English over false certainty.**

## Style / register

- Aim for elevated but readable English.
- Preserve repeated judgment formulas, consolatory reversals, visionary transitions, and covenantal language.
- Keep the rhetorical seriousness of divine justice, repentance, restoration, and eschatological reward.
- When a chapter edge or phrase is uncertain, prefer transparency plus footnote over overconfident paraphrase.
