# Book context — 1 Clement (Κλήμεντος πρὸς Κορινθίους)

## Author and audience

Composed in Rome, c. **96 CE** (most scholars date it to the end of
Domitian's reign). Traditionally attributed to **Clement**, presbyter
or bishop of Rome, though the letter itself is sent in the name of
"the church of God which sojourns in Rome" — a corporate authorial
voice, not a personal apostolic letter.

The audience is **the church at Corinth**, specifically a community
fractured by the deposition of duly-appointed presbyters by a
younger faction. The letter is a long, pastoral, sometimes severe
intervention urging restoration of the deposed elders, repentance of
the rebellious party, and renewed concord.

The register is **rhetorically formal Greek prose** — the author has
training in classical rhetoric (uses *enthymeme*, periodic syntax,
antithetical structures) and saturates the text with Septuagint
quotations and exempla (Cain, Abel, Moses, Rahab, Daniel, the
prophets, the apostles). It reads like a Greek deliberative oration
in epistolary form, **not** the colloquial Koine of Paul or the
catechetical brevity of the Didache.

## Source language

**Greek.** Two principal witnesses:
- **Codex Alexandrinus (5th century)** — incomplete; missing 57:7
  through 63:4 due to a lost folio.
- **Codex Hierosolymitanus H54 (1056 CE)** — same MS that preserves
  the Didache; supplies the missing Alexandrinus material.

Secondary witnesses: the Latin (Codex Florentinus + a Würzburg
manuscript), Syriac (12th century, complete), and Coptic fragments.

Our primary source: **Lightfoot 1889** critical edition, which
reconciles Alexandrinus and Hierosolymitanus and notes the secondary
versions.

## What the original audience would hear

- **A formal Roman intervention.** Corinthian Christians would hear
  this as Rome speaking with apostolic authority — though the letter
  carefully avoids any imperial tone, instead grounding its appeal in
  shared scripture and Christian humility. The English must preserve
  this **gravity without making it feel monarchic**.
- **A scripture-saturated argument.** The author quotes the LXX
  constantly, often without introduction formula, weaving prophetic
  oracles and psalmic phrases into his own prose. The original
  audience, immersed in the same LXX, would catch the resonances. An
  English translation that flattens these into generic religious
  language loses the author's primary mode of argument.
- **Exempla as proof.** Cain (envy → murder), Korah (rebellion →
  destruction), Rahab (humble repentance → salvation), Moses
  (gentle correction of Aaron and Miriam → restoration). The author
  expects the audience to recognize each story instantly and supply
  the moral inference. English must preserve the rhetorical force of
  the appeal without overexplaining.
- **A theology of order.** The letter argues that God appointed
  apostles, who appointed bishops and deacons in succession; the
  Corinthian rebellion is therefore **disorder against the divinely
  given order**. The keyword cluster — **τάξις** (order), **κανών**
  (rule), **ἀποστολικός** (apostolic), **διαδοχή** (succession) —
  carries the argument. The English must keep these visible and
  consistent, because the author's case stands or falls on this
  vocabulary.

## Translation challenges specific to this book

1. **Ἐπίσκοπος / πρεσβύτερος / διάκονος** — the technical vocabulary
   of early Christian order. The author uses *episkopos* and
   *presbyteros* somewhat interchangeably (1 Clem 44.1, 44.5),
   reflecting the pre-monarchical-bishop structure. The English must
   not retroject later "bishop / priest / deacon" English-Christian
   stratification onto these terms.
2. **Στάσις** (1.1, 2.6, 3.2 etc.) — the central problem word.
   "Sedition," "faction," "rebellion," "revolt," "discord" all carry
   different shades. In Greco-Roman political discourse στάσις was
   the antonym of *concord* (ὁμόνοια). The English must convey both
   the political weight (this is *civic* language applied to the
   church) and the spiritual seriousness.
3. **Πρόοδος / ἁρμονία / τάξις** in the cosmological sections (chs
   20, 33) — the author appeals to the order of the cosmos as model
   for ecclesial order. The English must let the cosmos-church
   parallel land.
4. **LXX-quote citation style.** The author often elides the
   citation formula, weaving the quoted material into his prose. The
   English should preserve quotational distinctness where the Greek
   uses recognizable LXX phrasing — but must not invent quotation
   marks where the Greek seamlessly merges them.
5. **Personification of cities and abstract nouns** (e.g., concord,
   strife, repentance) in the rhetorical passages. The English
   should let these stand as personified forces, not flatten them
   into modern abstract nouns.

## What NOT to import

- Later monarchical-episcopate ecclesiology.
- Reformation-era debates over apostolic succession.
- Modern Roman-Catholic / Orthodox / Protestant readings of the
  letter as proof-text for one polity over another.

The author is a Roman Christian elder writing in 96 CE to fix a
specific Corinthian crisis. Render what *that* author would have
meant his Corinthian audience to hear.
