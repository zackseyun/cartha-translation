# 2 Corinthians — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 13 chapters.
- **Form**: Pauline letter — possibly a composite of multiple letters. Chapters 1–9 are apologetic and pastoral; chapters 10–13 are sharply polemical (the "harsh letter"?).
- **Date**: ca. 55–56 AD from Macedonia.
- **Original audience**: The church at Corinth — Paul defending his apostleship against "super-apostles."

## Key translation issues

1. **Apostolic suffering vocabulary** (1:3–11; 4:7–18; 6:3–10; 11:23–33): The catalogues of suffering (peristasis catalogues) — "afflictions, hardships, calamities, beatings, imprisonments…" Render each item specifically, not summarized.
2. **παρακαλέω / παράκλησις** (1:3–7 — "comfort / consolation / encouragement"): Repeated 10× in 1:3–7. The repetition of the root is intentional. "Comfort" is traditional; keep the same root word consistently through the passage.
3. **"Thorn in the flesh" (σκόλοψ τῇ σαρκί, 12:7)**: Keep "thorn" — not "stake" or "splinter." "In the flesh" — not "for my body."
4. **The "super-apostles" (ὑπερλίαν ἀπόστολοι, 11:5; 12:11)**: Paul's sarcastic term. Keep the compound "super-apostles" — not "those greatest apostles."
5. **"New creation" (καινὴ κτίσις, 5:17)**: "If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation." Keep "new creation" — not "a new creature" (which misses the cosmic scope).
6. **The collection (chs. 8–9)**: Vocabulary for the relief collection — χάρις (grace/gift), εὐλογία (blessing/generous gift), λειτουργία (service/ministry). These terms carry their full theological weight even in financial contexts; do not strip them to merely financial language.
7. **"God loves a cheerful giver" (9:7)**: ἱλαρόν — "cheerful" is the traditional rendering. Keep it.

## Style / register

- Emotionally variable — Paul moves from anguish to joy to irony to exhortation within passages.
- Chapters 10–13 are aggressively ironic ("I am speaking as a fool…"). The irony must be preserved.

## Known pitfalls

- **Losing the repetition of παράκλησις** in 1:3–7 — vary it at your peril.
- **Softening the "fool's speech" irony** (11:1–12:13).
