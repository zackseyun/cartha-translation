# Prayer of Manasseh — book context for review and drafting

- **Work**: Prayer of Manasseh (Latin *Oratio Manasse*)
- **Form**: Pseudepigraphic penitential prayer, 15 verses, attributed to the repentant king Manasseh of Judah during his Babylonian captivity (cf. 2 Chron 33:11–13, 18–19)
- **Language**: Greek (though an underlying Hebrew/Aramaic original has been conjectured, no Semitic witness survives; treat the Greek as the source)
- **Likely date**: uncertain; most scholars place composition in the 2nd century BC – 1st century AD, Jewish-Hellenistic milieu
- **Primary critical edition for this project**: R.H. Charles, *The Apocrypha and Pseudepigrapha of the Old Testament in English*, Vol. 1 (Oxford, 1913), pp. 612–624, which reconstructs from:
  - Codex Alexandrinus (A) — principal witness
  - Codex Turicensis (T) — corroborating uncial
  - *Apostolic Constitutions* ii.22.12–14 — earliest Christian embedding
  - Didascalia Apostolorum (Syriac, Latin) — dependent tradition
- **Verse boundaries**: follow Charles's editorial numbering (15 verses; v.7 contains the known "insertion" that early Christian witnesses lack and which Charles's apparatus notes as omitted by A, T, *Const. Apost.*, Syr., Lat., Moz.; our Greek reconstruction omits it in line with A)
- **Original audience**: Jews shaped by Second Temple-era repentance theology, reading Manasseh's life as a case study of God's mercy toward even the worst sinner

## What matters most for translation

1. **Penitential register is liturgical, not narrative.** The Greek is structured as formal prayer — invocation (vv. 1–4), confession (vv. 5–10), petition (vv. 11–14), doxology (v. 15). Preserve the rhetorical shape; do not flatten into prose.
2. **The invocation chain in vv. 1–4 piles up divine epithets** (παντοκράτωρ, ὁ θεὸς τῶν πατέρων, ὁ ποιήσας τὸν οὐρανὸν καὶ τὴν γῆν, ὁ πεδήσας τὴν θάλασσαν, ὁ κλείσας τὴν ἄβυσσον). Each is weight-bearing — keep them as distinct clauses, not collapsed.
3. **Confession-language precision.** Distinguish:
   - ἀνομία (lawlessness) vs ἁμαρτία (sin) vs ἀδικία (unrighteousness) — all appear and are not interchangeable.
   - Codex Alexandrinus reads active ἐπλήθυναν ("my iniquities have multiplied") in v.9, not the Rahlfs passive ἐπληθύνθησαν ("have been multiplied"). Our Greek follows A. Translation must convey active agency.
4. **v.8 witness-split**: A reads simply ἐμοὶ τῷ ἁμαρτωλῷ ("to me the sinner"), NOT Rahlfs's ἐπ᾽ ἐμοὶ τῷ ἁμαρτωλῷ ("upon me the sinner"). The omission subtly changes register — keep it dative of advantage, not locative.
5. **Gendered covenant-formula** "God of our fathers…Abraham and Isaac and Jacob and of their righteous offspring" (v.1) is a fixed biblical formula; render with "fathers," not "ancestors."
6. **σπέρμα** in v.1 refers to the patriarchal covenant line — "offspring" preserves the singular-collective force; "seed" is too opaque for a contemporary reader; "descendants" too diffuse.
7. **Do not Christianize.** The prayer is entirely Jewish; no messianic language, no allusions to Christ. Any reading that sounds NT-shaped is almost certainly wrong.

## Style / register

- Elevated liturgical English; permissible to use "Thou" only if the surrounding corpus does, otherwise respectful second-person contemporary.
- Preserve the tight chain structure of the opening invocation (multiple aorist participles functioning attributively).
- The text moves from vast cosmic imagery to intimate confession (v.11 "Now I bend the knee of my heart") — preserve both registers cleanly.
- Avoid resolving ambiguity aggressively: the text's own rhetoric is cumulative and slightly repetitive; English should mirror that.

## Known translation pitfalls

- **παντοκράτωρ** → "Almighty" (project default; not "All-sovereign," not "Ruler of all" unless context specifically argues for it).
- **αἱ ἀνομίαι μου** (v.9) — "my lawless deeds" or "my iniquities"; avoid collapsing to generic "sins" which has a different Greek root.
- **τὸ ἔλεος** (v.7, v.14) — "mercy" consistently; God's covenant-loyalty dimension should be felt.
- **φρίξει** / τρέμει (v.4 "trembles") — cosmic fear before God, not human fright.
- **κλίνω γόνυ καρδίας μου** (v.11) — literally "I bend the knee of my heart." Preserve the metaphor verbatim; do not paraphrase to "I humble myself."
- **ἔθηκας μετάνοιαν** (v.8) — "you appointed repentance" (for the righteous who did not need it); translators often soften, but the theology of the verse depends on the paradox.
