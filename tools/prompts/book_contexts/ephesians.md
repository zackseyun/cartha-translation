# Ephesians — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 6 chapters.
- **Form**: Pauline letter (authorship debated — deutero-Pauline for many scholars). Structured as theology (1–3) then ethics (4–6). Opens with an extended berakah (blessing, 1:3–14) and prayer (1:15–23).
- **Date**: ca. 60 AD (if Pauline, during imprisonment) or ca. 80–90 AD (if deutero-Pauline).
- **Original audience**: Possibly a circular letter to multiple churches in Asia Minor (some MSS lack "in Ephesus").

## Key translation issues

1. **The long opening blessing** (1:3–14): A single sentence in Greek (or very few, depending on punctuation). Render it to preserve the cumulative, doxological sweep — do not break it into too many short sentences that lose the flow.
2. **πλήρωμα** (1:23; 3:19; 4:13 — "fullness"): The "fullness" of Christ / God filling all things. Always "fullness" — not "completeness" or "totality."
3. **τὰ ἐπουράνια** ("the heavenly places / heavenly realms"): Ephesus's distinctive phrase (1:3, 20; 2:6; 3:10; 6:12). "Heavenly places" is the traditional rendering; "heavenly realms" is a modern equivalent. Pick one and keep it consistent across all five occurrences.
4. **κεφαλή** ("head"): Christ as "head" of the church and husband as "head" of the wife (1:22; 4:15; 5:23). The metaphor must be rendered literally — "head" — allowing readers to interpret the significance. Do not substitute "authority" or "leader."
5. **The household code (5:22–6:9)**: Wives/husbands, children/parents, slaves/masters. Render the instructions as the text gives them, without softening or adding qualifiers not in the Greek. "Slaves, obey your masters" — "slaves," not "servants."
6. **The armor of God** (6:10–20): Each piece of armor — ζώνη (belt), θώραξ (breastplate), ὑποδήματα (shoes), ἀσπίς (shield), περικεφαλαία (helmet), μάχαιρα (sword). Render the military vocabulary precisely and consistently.
7. **μυστήριον** ("mystery"): Ephesians uses "mystery" for the previously hidden plan of God now revealed in Christ. Always "mystery" — not "secret" (which loses the apocalyptic disclosure sense).

## Style / register

- Elevated, doxological, almost liturgical in chapters 1–3. The long sentences are deliberate.
- Chapters 4–6 shift to practical paraenesis — direct and imperative.

## Known pitfalls

- **Breaking up the long blessing (1:3–14)** into too many short sentences, destroying the cumulative doxology.
- **Rendering κεφαλή as "authority"** — that is interpretation, not translation.
- **Softening "slaves"** to "servants" in the household code.
