# Obadiah — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (MT). LXX (Rahlfs) for variants. Close parallels with Jeremiah 49:7–22.
- **Form**: Prophetic oracle against Edom. One chapter (21 verses) — the shortest book in the Hebrew Bible. Oracle format with second-person address to Edom.
- **Date**: Likely post-exilic, after the fall of Jerusalem (586 BC). Edom's role in that catastrophe (aiding Babylonians, looting refugees — cf. Ps 137:7; Lam 4:21–22) is the immediate grievance. Some place it earlier.
- **Original audience**: Judah in or after the exilic period, seeking reassurance that Edom's treachery will be judged.

## Key translation issues

1. **גֵּאוֹן לִבְּךָ** (v. 3 — "the pride of your heart"): the oracle's opening charge. גֵּאוֹן is more than pride — it is arrogance backed by presumed security. "The arrogance of your heart" is accurate.
2. **The "Day of the LORD" section** (vv. 15–21): shifts from Edom-specific oracle to universal eschatological vision. The phrase יוֹם-יהוה must be rendered consistently with other prophetic books — "the Day of the LORD."
3. **אַחֶיךָ** (v. 10, 12 — "your brother"): Edom = Esau; Israel = Jacob. The "brotherly" relationship is the aggravating factor in Edom's treachery. Make the kinship language explicit.
4. **Parallels with Jeremiah 49**: Obadiah shares extensive verbal overlap with Jeremiah 49:7–22 (both directions of borrowing are debated). The COB translation should render each text on its own terms; do not harmonize wording.

## Style / register

- Compact, dense oracle poetry. Every line carries weight.
- The second-person address to Edom (vv. 2–16) should keep the directness and accusatory force of the Hebrew.
- The closing vision (vv. 17–21) shifts to third-person promise; the register lifts into eschatological proclamation.

## Known pitfalls

- **Underestimating the force of the text's brevity.** The density of Obadiah rewards close attention to each word.
- **Flattening the transition from historical oracle (vv. 1–14) to eschatological vision (vv. 15–21).** The shift is a key structural move.
