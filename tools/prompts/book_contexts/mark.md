# Mark — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 16 chapters.
- **Form**: Gospel narrative. The shortest and earliest Gospel. Rapid, action-oriented narrative with frequent use of εὐθύς ("immediately"). Often considered the primary source used by Matthew and Luke (Marcan priority).
- **Date**: ca. 65–70 AD, possibly during or just before the Jewish-Roman War.
- **Original audience**: Roman Christians (possibly), Gentile audience assumed — Jewish customs and Aramaic phrases are explained.

## Key translation issues

1. **εὐθύς / εὐθέως** ("immediately / at once"): Mark's signature word, used 42× in the Gospel. Preserve it every time — do not dilute to "then" or "and." The narrative pace is deliberate.
2. **εὐαγγέλιον** (1:1 — "the gospel / the good news"): Mark opens with "The beginning of the gospel of Jesus Christ, the Son of God." "Gospel" is traditional and correct; preserve it.
3. **Aramaic phrases** (5:41; 7:34; 15:34): "Talitha koum," "Ephphatha," "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani" — always give the Aramaic first, then Mark's own translation. Do not omit either.
4. **ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου** ("the Son of Man"): Mark's preferred self-designation for Jesus. Always "the Son of Man" — not "the human being" or "the son of a human."
5. **Messianic Secret terminology**: "He ordered them to tell no one" — render these commands to silence literally; they are a key Marcan theological motif.
6. **ἐκπλήσσω / θαμβέω / ἐκθαμβέω** ("amazed / astonished / filled with awe"): Mark's crowd-reaction vocabulary. Distinguish these from simple "surprise."
7. **Sandwiched pericopes (intercalations)**: Mark frequently "sandwiches" one story inside another. Narrative transitions at the joins must be clear.

## Style / register

- Rapid, paratactic (καί-heavy), colloquial. Mark writes at a lower literary register than Luke.
- Preserve the roughness: many paratactic "and… and… and" constructions. Do not subordinate clauses that Mark leaves coordinate.
- Direct speech is vivid and immediate.
- The abrupt ending (16:8) at "for they were afraid" is almost certainly the original end — the longer ending (16:9–20) is a secondary addition; mark in the text if footnotes are available.

## Known pitfalls

- **Smoothing εὐθύς into "then."** The urgency is the point.
- **Harmonizing Mark to Matthew or Luke.** Mark's shorter, rougher versions are original to Mark.
- **Over-rendering the Aramaic** — give it once with Mark's own translation; do not paraphrase.
