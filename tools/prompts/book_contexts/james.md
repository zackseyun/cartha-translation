# James — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 5 chapters.
- **Form**: Epistle, though more like a homily or wisdom collection. Closest NT book to OT wisdom literature. Addressed "to the twelve tribes in the Dispersion."
- **Date**: ca. 45–65 AD. If by James the brother of Jesus, it may be the earliest NT letter.
- **Original audience**: Jewish Christians, possibly in a broad diaspora context.

## Key translation issues

1. **πίστις / ἔργα** ("faith / works"): The faith-works discussion (2:14–26) is the theological center. "Can faith save him?" (2:14) — πίστις here is dead, inactive faith. Render precisely; do not harmonize with Paul's use (Paul uses "works of the law"; James uses "works" as concrete acts of mercy).
2. **νόμος τέλειος τῆς ἐλευθερίας** (1:25; 2:12 — "the perfect law of liberty / freedom"): "The perfect law of liberty." Keep both adjectives.
3. **πειρασμός / πειράζω** ("trial / temptation / testing"): James uses the same root for external trials (1:2–4) and internal temptation (1:13–15). The wordplay is intentional. Render "trial" for the external testing and "temptation" for the internal enticement — but keep a footnote to the shared root.
4. **γεέννα** (3:6 — "hell / Gehenna"): "The tongue is set on fire by Gehenna." Keep "Gehenna" — not "hell" (which loses the geographical/eschatological specificity) — or at minimum footnote it.
5. **The rich and poor contrast** (2:1–13; 5:1–6): Renders James's social critique sharply. "Come now, you rich, weep and howl…" (5:1) — do not soften.
6. **δίψυχος** (1:8; 4:8 — "double-minded"): A word possibly coined by James (NT hapax). Keep "double-minded."
7. **εὐχή / εὔχομαι** (5:15–16 — "prayer / to pray"): Distinguished from προσευχή in the same passage. Footnote if the distinction matters; usually both render as "prayer."

## Style / register

- Direct, earthy, moralistic. James does not philosophize — he commands.
- The wisdom aphorisms (1:19: "Be quick to hear, slow to speak, slow to anger") are crisp and memorable.
- Chapter 5:1–6 is a prophetic invective — keep the rhetorical force.

## Known pitfalls

- **Harmonizing James's "works" with Paul's "works of the law."** They are different discussions.
- **Softening the rebuke of the rich** (5:1–6) — the harshness is the point.
- **Rendering δίψυχος as "indecisive"** or "wavering" — "double-minded" preserves the image of a divided self.
