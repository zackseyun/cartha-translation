# Job — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 42 chapters.
- **Form**: Prose frame (1–2; 42:7–17) enclosing poetry (3–41). The poetry includes Job's laments, the three friends' speeches, Elihu's speeches (32–37), and the divine speeches from the whirlwind (38–41).
- **Date**: Difficult to date. The story may be ancient; the poetic composition is often dated ca. 6th–4th cent. BC.
- **Original audience**: The wisdom tradition's meditation on innocent suffering and divine justice.

## Key translation issues

1. **The prose frame vs. the poetry**: The prose (chs. 1–2; 42:7–17) is simple, direct narrative. The poetry is some of the most difficult Hebrew in the Bible — dense, archaic, hapax-heavy. The contrast in register is intentional; preserve it.
2. **Hapax legomena**: Job contains more unique words than almost any other biblical book. Where the meaning is uncertain, prefer an honest rendering over a smooth guess. Footnote key uncertainties.
3. **שָׂטָן** (1:6–2:10): In the prose frame, הַשָּׂטָן = "the satan" (the accuser, an adversarial role before God's court). Not yet the proper name "Satan." Render "the adversary" or "the accuser" with the article — not "Satan."
4. **חִנָּם** (1:9; 2:3 — "for nothing / without cause"): "Does Job fear God for nothing?" — key to the book's theme. Keep "for nothing" or "without cause."
5. **גֹּאֵל** (19:25 — "redeemer / vindicator"): "I know that my Redeemer lives." גֹּאֵל is a legal kinsman-redeemer. "Redeemer" is traditional; "vindicator" captures the legal dimension. Either is defensible; footnote.
6. **The divine speeches** (38–41): YHWH's questions are rhetorical interrogatives — "Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?" Do not flatten them to statements. The grammar is insistent interrogation.
7. **לִוְיָתָן / בְּהֵמוֹת** (40:15; 41:1 — "Behemoth / Leviathan"): Mythological creatures. Keep the transliterated names "Behemoth" and "Leviathan" — do not translate as "hippopotamus" or "crocodile."

## Style / register

- Prose frame: simple, patterned, almost ritualistic ("the LORD gave, and the LORD has taken away"). Preserve the simplicity.
- Poetry: sublime, dense, archaic. The diction is deliberately difficult and elevated. Do not smooth it into easy modern prose.
- The friends' speeches follow discernible rhetorical patterns; Job's responses are more anguished and direct.

## Known pitfalls

- **Over-smoothing the poetic difficulty.** Obscurity in the Hebrew should surface as difficulty in the English, not false clarity.
- **Capitalizing "Redeemer" in 19:25** without noting it is an interpretive choice — the text does not necessarily point to Christ.
- **Rendering "the satan" as a proper name.** He is a role/function in the heavenly court, not yet the New Testament figure.
