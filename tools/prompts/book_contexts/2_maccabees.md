# 2 Maccabees — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Hanhart). Composed in Greek (unlike 1 Maccabees). An epitome (condensation) of a five-volume work by Jason of Cyrene. 15 chapters.
- **Form**: Hellenistic historiography with pathetic (emotional) rhetoric, epitome format. Covers some of the same ground as 1 Maccabees 1–7, but with different emphases.
- **Date**: Epitome probably 1st c. BC, before 63 BC. The two prefatory letters (1:1–9; 1:10–2:18) may be earlier.
- **Original audience**: Greek-speaking Jews, possibly in Egypt. The book emphasizes resurrection, martyrdom, and the intercession of the dead.

## Key translation issues

1. **Style contrast with 1 Maccabees**: 2 Maccabees is rhetorical, emotionally charged Hellenistic prose. The author explicitly apologizes for the condensation and invites reader engagement. Render with appropriate Hellenistic literary register, not the flat chronicle style of 1 Maccabees.
2. **ἀνάστασις** ("resurrection"): the martyrdom accounts (chs. 6–7) contain explicit resurrection hope ("the King of the world will raise us up to an everlasting renewal of life," 7:9). This is one of the clearest pre-NT resurrection statements. Render "resurrection" (not "revival" or "raising up") consistently.
3. **The Martyrdom accounts** (6:18–7:42): graphic and intense. The mother of seven sons and her sons are tortured and killed. Render the martyrdom accounts with their full emotional weight — the Greek is visceral.
4. **τιμωρία / κόλασις** ("punishment"): divine punishment for sin, understood as corrective (6:12–17). Keep the distinction between punitive and corrective punishment where the text makes it.
5. **Intercession of the dead** (12:39–45): Judas's prayer for the fallen soldiers who had idolatrous amulets — one of the deuterocanonical foundations for prayers for the dead. Render the theological logic of the passage precisely; do not editorialize.
6. **The Epistolary Prefaces** (1:1–9; 1:10–2:18): formal official letters; render with official register.

## Style / register

- Emotional, rhetorical, Hellenistic. The author uses pathos consciously.
- The martyrdom accounts are high-register narrative; they should have dramatic weight.
- The diplomatic/religious prefaces are formal prose.
- The author's preface (2:19–32) and conclusion (15:37–39) are self-conscious literary meta-commentary; keep that tone.

## Known pitfalls

- **Toning down the graphic violence** in chs. 6–7. The author chose visceral detail deliberately; the translation should not be more modest.
- **Importing 1 Maccabees' flat chronicle style.** These are different books with different literary aims.
