# Ezekiel — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 48 chapters.
- **Form**: Prophetic visions, oracles, sign-acts, allegories, and the concluding temple vision. The most systematically organized of the major prophets. Contains some of the most extravagant symbolic imagery in the Bible.
- **Date**: Ezekiel's ministry ca. 593–573 BC in Babylon (after the first deportation). He was among the exiles.
- **Original audience**: Judean exiles in Babylon.

## Key translation issues

1. **בֶּן אָדָם** ("son of man / mortal"): God addresses Ezekiel as בֶּן אָדָם throughout (~93×). This means "mortal" or "human being" — contrast with the divine. "Son of man" is traditional; "mortal" or "O mortal" (NRSV) is more interpretively precise. Either is defensible; pick one and footnote the alternative.
2. **The throne-chariot vision (merkavah, ch. 1)**: The vision of the four living creatures, wheels within wheels, the firmament, and the divine glory. Render the imagery literally — do not decode it in the text. "Something like the appearance of…" (כְּמַרְאֵה) — Ezekiel uses these hedging approximation phrases throughout; they are theologically significant (he is careful not to claim direct vision of God). Keep "something like."
3. **כָּבוֹד יְהוָה** ("the glory of the LORD"): Central to Ezekiel's theology — the glory departs the temple (10–11) and returns (43). Always "the glory of the LORD" — consistent rendering essential.
4. **The dry bones vision (ch. 37)**: "Can these bones live?" — rhetorical question; keep it. The two-stage revival (sinews, flesh, breath) — render the sequence precisely.
5. **The allegories** (16, 17, 23): Jerusalem as unfaithful wife (16; 23), the eagle and the vine (17). These allegories use explicit sexual language that should be rendered accurately, not sanitized.
6. **גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג** (38–39 — "Gog and Magog"): Symbolic enemies. Keep "Gog" and "Magog" as proper names — do not attempt to identify them in the text.
7. **The temple vision (40–48)**: Highly technical architectural and legal specifications. Render dimensions, gates, rooms, and priestly regulations accurately and consistently.

## Style / register

- Vision reports: intricate, recursive, visionary — the language spirals and hedges. Preserve the careful approximation.
- Oracles: often harsh and graphic. Do not soften.
- The allegories (16; 23) are deliberately shocking — they use sexual metaphor as prophetic rhetoric. Render faithfully.

## Known pitfalls

- **Decoding the merkavah imagery** in the translation text. Render the vision; leave interpretation to footnotes.
- **Sanitizing the sexual language** in chapters 16 and 23. It is intentionally provocative prophetic rhetoric.
- **Inconsistent rendering of כָּבוֹד** — it must be "glory" every time, not varied.
