# Prayer of Azariah and Song of the Three Young Men — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs — either Theodotion or Old Greek; Theodotion is more widely attested). An addition inserted into Daniel 3 after verse 23 in the LXX.
- **Form**: Two liturgical compositions: (1) The Prayer of Azariah (vv. 1–22): penitential prayer in the fiery furnace. (2) The Song of the Three Young Men / Benedicite (vv. 29–68): a hymn calling all creation to bless God (akin to Psalm 148, and later used as a canticle in Christian liturgy).
- **Date**: Composition uncertain; possibly 2nd c. BC.
- **Original audience**: Jews who used these compositions liturgically; found in LXX Daniel.

## Key translation issues

1. **The Prayer of Azariah** (vv. 1–22): a deuteronomic confessional prayer, structured as: acknowledgment of God's justice → confession of sin → plea for mercy. Render with the liturgical-penitential register appropriate to this well-established prayer form.
2. **The Song of the Three** (vv. 29–68): the Benedicite. This is a sustained anaphoric hymn — "Bless the Lord, [X], bless him and exalt him above all forever." The refrain recurs after each group addressed. It MUST be rendered with the exact same refrain each time — this is a liturgical composition with fixed form.
3. **The creation catalog**: the hymn calls upon angels, heavens, waters, sun/moon/stars, rain/dew, winds, fire/heat, cold/ice, nights/days, light/darkness, lightnings, clouds, mountains/hills, plants, seas, rivers, beasts, birds, all people, Israel, priests, righteous ones. Each category should be specifically named — do not abbreviate or compress.
4. **εὐλογητός / αἰνεῖτε αὐτόν** ("Blessed / Praise him"): the refrain "Bless him and exalt him above all forever" — render the exact same wording each time.

## Style / register

- Liturgical throughout. Both compositions are formal worship texts.
- The Prayer: meditative, penitential, corporate.
- The Song: processional, joyful, cosmic. The cumulative listing is the rhetorical mode; rushing it loses the point.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying the refrain** for English diversity. Do not. It is a liturgical formula.
- **Condensing the creation catalog.** The comprehensiveness is the theology — all creation praising God.
