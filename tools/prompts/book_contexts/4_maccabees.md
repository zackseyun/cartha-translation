# 4 Maccabees — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs). Composed in Greek. 18 chapters.
- **Form**: Philosophical diatribe / rhetorical speech with narrative illustration. The thesis: "devout reason is sovereign over the emotions" (1:1). Illustrates this thesis with the martyrdom narratives from 2 Maccabees (Eleazar, the seven brothers, their mother).
- **Date**: 1st c. BC–1st c. AD. Probably Antioch or Alexandria.
- **Original audience**: Hellenized Jews who valued both Greek philosophy and Jewish piety. The book synthesizes Stoic philosophy with Jewish covenant theology.

## Key translation issues

1. **The philosophical vocabulary**: The thesis statement (1:1) uses Stoic terminology — εὐσεβής λογισμός (devout/pious reason), πάθη (emotions/passions). These are technical philosophical terms. Render with philosophical precision:
   - λογισμός: "reason" or "the mind" (not just "thoughts")
   - πάθη: "emotions" or "passions" (the Stoic technical term for desires that reason must master)
   - σωφροσύνη: "self-control" (the cardinal virtue)
2. **The martyrdom rhetoric**: More explicitly philosophical and deliberate than in 2 Maccabees. The martyrs argue philosophically before dying. Their speeches are formal rhetorical compositions; render with rhetorical sophistication.
3. **ἀθανασία** ("immortality"): the martyrs' hope is immortality, not bodily resurrection (contrast 2 Maccabees). The philosophical immortality of the soul vs. the resurrection of the body is a real distinction; the text takes the philosophical position. Render accurately — "immortality" not "resurrection."
4. **The mother's speech** (chs. 15–16): she is presented as the model of rational self-control over maternal emotion. Her philosophical argument is the book's climax; render with full rhetorical weight.
5. **λύτρον / ἀντίψυχον** (17:21–22 — "ransom / sacrifice on behalf of"): atoning significance of the martyrs' deaths. Important language that later Christian theology picks up. Render precisely.

## Style / register

- Highly rhetorical, oratorical Hellenistic Greek. The book is effectively a formal rhetorical speech (diatribe).
- Philosophical vocabulary should be rendered with philosophical precision.
- The martyrdom narrative sections are deliberately dramatic and emotional, even while arguing for reason's mastery over emotion — the irony is intentional.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening philosophical vocabulary to common English.** The technical precision is the point.
- **Conflating 4 Maccabees' immortality-of-the-soul with 2 Maccabees' resurrection theology.** They have different positions; each should be rendered on its own terms.
