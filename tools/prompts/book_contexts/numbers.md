# Numbers — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 36 chapters.
- **Form**: Mixed narrative and law. Census lists (1–4; 26), wilderness narratives (10–25), various legal collections interspersed. The book's Hebrew name is בְּמִדְבַּר ("In the Wilderness").
- **Date**: Final priestly editing likely post-exilic, core traditions much older.
- **Original audience**: Israel's identity in the wilderness — between Sinai and Canaan.

## Key translation issues

1. **Census/counting vocabulary**: פָּקַד ("to number / count / muster") — the verb behind the book's English title "Numbers." Render "number" or "muster" consistently in the census contexts.
2. **The tribal structure**: The twelve tribes and their precise census counts must be rendered consistently. Tribal names as proper nouns should be stable throughout.
3. **נָשִׂיא** ("leader / prince / chief"): The tribal leader designation. "Leader" or "chief" — not "prince" (which has monarchic connotations inappropriate here).
4. **The Aaronic Benediction** (6:24–26): "The LORD bless you and keep you; the LORD make his face shine on you and be gracious to you; the LORD turn his face toward you and give you peace." This is the most quoted passage in Numbers. Render with the full weight of a liturgical blessing — the threefold structure must be preserved exactly.
5. **The oracles of Balaam** (22–24): Poetry embedded in narrative. The four oracles (23:7–10, 18–24; 24:3–9, 15–19) must be rendered as poetry. The famous "star out of Jacob" (24:17) — keep "star."
6. **The rebellion accounts**: Multiple wilderness complaints (11; 14; 16; 20; 21). Each follows a pattern (complaint → divine anger → Moses's intercession → judgment). Preserve the formulaic structure.
7. **כָּלֵב / יְהוֹשֻׁעַ** (Caleb/Joshua): proper names — transliterate consistently.

## Style / register

- Varied: dry census lists, dramatic narrative, embedded poetry, and legal material.
- The spy narrative (13–14) and Korah's rebellion (16) are gripping narrative — preserve the tension.
- Balaam's oracles are among the most elevated poetry in the Pentateuch — render as verse.

## Known pitfalls

- **Flattening the Balaam oracles** into prose — they are formally marked poetry.
- **Varying the Aaronic Benediction wording** — it is a fixed liturgical text.
- **Inconsistent tribal name spelling** across the census lists.
