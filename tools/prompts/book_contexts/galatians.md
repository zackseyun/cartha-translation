# Galatians — book context for review

- **Source**: Nestle-Aland 28 / SBL Greek NT. 6 chapters.
- **Form**: Pauline letter — polemical, urgent, autobiographical. Paul defends his apostolic authority and the gospel of grace against a "different gospel" demanding circumcision.
- **Date**: ca. 48–55 AD (debated — North or South Galatian theory affects date).
- **Original audience**: Gentile Christians in Galatia being pressured to accept circumcision and Torah observance.

## Key translation issues

1. **εὐαγγέλιον / ἕτερον εὐαγγέλιον** (1:6–9): "A different gospel — which is really no gospel at all." The double anathema must be rendered with full force: "Let him be accursed" (ἀνάθεμα ἔστω). Do not soften.
2. **δικαιοσύνη / δικαιόω / πίστις** (the same cluster as Romans): See Romans context. Galatians is where this vocabulary is most polemically deployed. The πίστις Χριστοῦ debate (2:16; 3:22) — footnote both options (faith in Christ / faithfulness of Christ).
3. **ἔργα νόμου** ("works of the law"): Paul's polemical phrase — not "doing good deeds" in general, but specifically the practice of Torah observance (circumcision, dietary laws, calendar). Render "works of the law" — do not expand to "works of the Law of Moses" or collapse to "legalism."
4. **ἐλευθερία / ἐλεύθερος** ("freedom / free"): Chapter 5 — "For freedom Christ has set us free." The repetition of the root is emphatic; keep it in English ("For freedom, Christ has freed us" or preserve "free/freedom").
5. **The allegory of Hagar and Sarah** (4:21–31): Paul calls it an ἀλληγορούμενα ("allegory"). Do not flatten the two-covenant typology. "Which things are an allegory" — keep "allegory."
6. **"In Christ there is neither Jew nor Greek…"** (3:28): Render precisely — "neither… nor… neither… nor…" — the threefold parallel structure is intentional.
7. **καρπὸς τοῦ πνεύματος** (5:22–23 — "fruit of the Spirit"): Nine qualities listed as a single "fruit" (singular). Keep "fruit" (singular) — not "fruits."

## Style / register

- Hot, polemical, urgent. Paul is angry. Chapters 1–2 are autobiographical combat.
- The autobiographical section (1:11–2:21) uses vivid personal narrative, including the confrontation at Antioch (2:11–14).
- Chapters 5–6 are paraenesis — practical moral instruction.

## Known pitfalls

- **Softening the anathema** (1:8–9). "Let him be condemned" loses the cultic exclusion weight of ἀνάθεμα.
- **Resolving the πίστις Χριστοῦ debate silently** — footnote both options.
- **Rendering "works of the law" as "earning salvation"** — this is an interpretive gloss, not a translation.
