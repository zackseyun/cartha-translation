# 2 Kings — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 25 chapters.
- **Form**: Narrative — continuation of 1 Kings. Elijah's translation, Elisha's ministry, fall of Israel (722 BC), Hezekiah and Isaiah, Josiah's reform, fall of Jerusalem (586 BC).
- **Date**: Deuteronomistic editing completed after 586 BC.
- **Original audience**: Explanation of the disaster of exile — the theological history of why Jerusalem fell.

## Key translation issues

1. **The Deuteronomistic regnal formulas** (continued from 1 Kings): "He did evil in the sight of the LORD, walking in the ways of Jeroboam son of Nebat." Keep the exact wording consistent with 1 Kings.
2. **Elijah's translation** (2:11): "And Elijah went up by a whirlwind into heaven." Keep "whirlwind" and "heaven" — these are specific and traditional.
3. **Elisha's double portion** (2:9): "Please let a double portion of your spirit be on me." "Double portion" — not "twice your spirit."
4. **The Shunammite's son** (4:8–37) and **Naaman** (5:1–19): These are two of the great miracle narratives in the OT. The dialogue in both is distinctive and should be rendered vividly.
5. **Hezekiah's prayer** (19:15–19 // Isaiah 37:15–20): "O LORD, God of Israel, enthroned above the cherubim, you are the God, you alone, of all the kingdoms of the earth." Render with full dignity of royal prayer.
6. **The fall of Jerusalem** (25): Terse, controlled narration of catastrophe. The brevity is part of the horror — do not expand or editorialize.
7. **"As the LORD had spoken through his servants the prophets"**: The theological refrain explaining events as fulfillment of prophetic warning. Keep the formula consistent.

## Style / register

- Royal chronicle framework with vivid embedded narratives.
- The Elisha cycle (2:1–8:15) has an episodic, almost hagiographic quality.
- The fall of Jerusalem (25) is devastatingly understated — preserve the restraint.

## Known pitfalls

- **Varying the regnal evaluation formula** from 1 Kings.
- **Adding emotional weight to the fall of Jerusalem (25)** — the restraint is the literary choice.
