# 2 Samuel — book context for review

- **Source**: Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC) / Unicode Hebrew Bible (UHB). 24 chapters.
- **Form**: Narrative — David's reign from his accession (1–8) through domestic and political crises (9–20), with appendices (21–24).
- **Date**: Deuteronomistic final editing 7th–6th cent. BC.
- **Original audience**: Israel's memory of the Davidic monarchy — its glory and its moral failures.

## Key translation issues

1. **The Davidic Covenant** (7:8–16): "Your house and your kingdom shall be made sure forever before me." The covenant language — בַּיִת (house/dynasty), כִּסֵּא (throne), לְעוֹלָם (forever) — must be rendered precisely. "Forever" — not "for a long time."
2. **חֶסֶד** in the David-Mephibosheth narrative (9): "Is there still anyone left of the house of Saul, that I may show him kindness (חֶסֶד) for Jonathan's sake?" — covenant loyalty. Keep "kindness" or "steadfast love."
3. **Nathan's parable** (12:1–4): "There were two men in one city…" The parable is a rhetorical trap. Preserve the simplicity of the story and the abruptness of David's judgment.
4. **Absalom's rebellion** (13–20): The political and family betrayal narrative. "The king is deeply moved" (18:33: "O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom!") — the grief repetition must be preserved literally.
5. **David's lament for Saul and Jonathan** (1:19–27): "How the mighty have fallen!" — a dirge of great literary quality. Render as poetry with the full weight of warrior-elegiac form.
6. **The Song of David** (22:1–51): Nearly identical to Psalm 18. Render as poetry. The two versions may differ slightly — render the 2 Samuel text as given, not harmonized to Psalm 18.
7. **The census and its consequences** (24): "The anger of the LORD was kindled against Israel" — the narrative's theological framing should be preserved without rationalization.

## Style / register

- The "Succession Narrative" (9–20) is considered among the finest ancient prose narrative — psychological depth, political intrigue, moral complexity.
- David's lament (1:19–27) is formal elegiac poetry — render with full poetic register.
- Narrative is often oblique — the narrator implies rather than states. Do not over-explain.

## Known pitfalls

- **Importing David's grief speech (18:33) as a summary** — the repetition of "my son" must be rendered exactly.
- **Harmonizing the Song of David (22) with Psalm 18** — translate the actual 2 Samuel text.
