# Psalms of Solomon — book context for review and drafting

- **Work**: Psalms of Solomon (*Psalmi Salomonis*, Ψαλμοὶ Σαλωμῶντος)
- **Form**: 18 Jewish psalms in Greek, composed in Second Temple Jerusalem, preserved outside the canonical Psalter
- **Language**: Greek — but almost certainly translated from a lost Hebrew (or possibly Aramaic) original. Linguistic Semitisms pervade the Greek.
- **Date**: c. 63–40 BC. Psalm 2 explicitly describes Pompey's conquest of Jerusalem (63 BC) and his death in Egypt (48 BC). Most psalms were likely composed within a generation of these events.
- **Historical setting**: Pharisaic-adjacent pious circles opposed to the later Hasmonean monarchy (non-Davidic, non-Zadokite) and horrified by Roman/Gentile desecration of the temple.
- **Primary critical edition for this project**: Henry B. Swete, *The Old Testament in Greek According to the Septuagint*, Vol. 3 (Cambridge, 1894; appendix). Our source is the Swete LXX 1909 text.
- **Also consulted**: R.H. Charles, APOT Vol. 2 (1913), pp. 625–652 (Gray's translation + Ryle & James / von Gebhardt apparatus). This is a pre-modern scholarly reference; can be cited but is not decisive.
- **Witnesses**: 11 Greek manuscripts (J, C, H, L, R, V, etc.) + a Syriac version (S) derived from the Greek. Real manuscript variants — not all "witnesses" agree.
- **Verse numbering**: we follow the Swete numbering (50 verses in Ps. 17). Charles prints both Swete's and von Gebhardt's older numbering (which has 51 in Ps. 17); where the two diverge, cite Swete.
- **Original audience**: Jerusalem-area pious Jews of the late Hasmonean / early Roman period, steeped in the canonical Psalter, the Prophets, and the language of covenant renewal.

## What matters most for translation

1. **These are psalms — preserve the parallelism.** Hebrew-style bicola and tricola dominate. The Greek retains them. Translation must not flatten into continuous prose.
2. **"Sinners" (ἁμαρτωλοί) and "righteous" (δίκαιοι) are group-labels, not abstractions.**
   - "Sinners" in PsSol usually = non-Davidic Hasmonean rulers + their priestly/political supporters (the Sadducean faction).
   - "Righteous" = the pious community on the other side (Pharisaic / Essene-adjacent).
   - NEVER read generic modern "good people / bad people"; the sociology is specific.
3. **"The lawless one" / "the man that was alien" (17:13, 17:9) = Pompey.** These are historical allusions, not eschatological. Preserve Hebrew idiom. Do not Christianize into "the Antichrist."
4. **The Son of David / Messiah of Ps 17 is the locus classicus for Second Temple messianism.**
   - 17:21 *ἀνάστησον αὐτοῖς τὸν βασιλέα αὐτῶν υἱὸν Δαυίδ* — "raise up their king, son of David" — the definite messianic-title force is in play even when the article is absent (Hebraic idiom via Greek).
   - 17:32 *ὁ χριστὸς κύριος* ("the anointed of the Lord" / variant "the Lord Messiah") — the textual status of κύριος is disputed; Charles and Rahlfs read genitive κυρίου. Be careful with the punctuation and case.
   - Preserve the Jewish political-messiah force. The Messiah of PsSol 17 purges Jerusalem, gathers the tribes, shepherds God's people — a non-militaristic righteous king, but *not* yet the Christian suffering-servant paradigm.
5. **Temple-language is real.** Allusions to the First and Second Temple cult, the Davidic throne, the "holy courts" — these are concrete places in the author's world.
6. **Hebrew substrate leaks through.**
   - Greek ἴδες ("you saw," 17:21) = Hebrew *rā'āh* "choose/provide" (cf. Gen 22:8, 1 Sam 16:1) — not literal seeing. Translate to the sense, footnote the literal.
   - Circumstantial constructions often mask a Hebrew waw-consecutive; don't translate them as English present participles if an indicative past fits.
7. **Do NOT harmonize with canonical Psalms.** Echoes of the Davidic Psalter are abundant but PsSol has its own angle (polemic against Hasmoneans, specific eschatological program). Preserve divergence.

## Style / register

- Hebrew-shaped Greek → elevated, psalmic English. Retain bicolon indentation if the drafter or reviewer can signal it; do not collapse lines.
- Theological weight: words like χάρις, ἔλεος, δικαιοσύνη, ἁμαρτία, ὅσιοι, πιστοί carry the same semantic fields as the canonical LXX; translate consistently with the rest of the project's LXX vocabulary.
- Register shifts: lament (Pss. 1, 2, 8, 17 opening), didactic wisdom (3, 4, 13, 14), thanksgiving (16), messianic hope (17–18). The English should carry these shifts audibly.

## Known translation pitfalls

- **ὁ πατήρ** (2:3, 13:9, 18:4) is Israel's Father in the covenantal sense, not a personal prayer-address; avoid "Daddy" or intimate rendering.
- **ὅσιοι** ≠ ἅγιοι exactly. "Pious" or "faithful ones" tracks better than "holy ones."
- **παιδεία / παιδεύω** — discipline/chastisement, NOT education in the modern sense. Covenant correction.
- **ὀργή θυμοῦ** — "wrath of indignation"; Hebraic doubled noun. Do not translate as "angry wrath" (pleonastic in English); prefer one strong noun or preserve the doubled force deliberately.
- **Tool-call residue**: 18 verses in our current PsSol draft were saved as raw `submit_verse_draft({"english_text":"…"})` JSON wrappers instead of plain English. These will be redrafted before this review pass; reviewers should expect clean text only.
