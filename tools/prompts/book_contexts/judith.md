# Judith — book context for review

- **Source**: LXX (Rahlfs / Hanhart critical edition). No Hebrew original survives; the book was likely composed in Hebrew/Aramaic and translated, or composed directly in Greek. 16 chapters.
- **Form**: Novelistic narrative. First half (chs. 1–7): Assyrian military campaign, Israelite resistance. Second half (chs. 8–16): Judith's infiltration and assassination of Holofernes.
- **Date**: The historical setting is Assyrian (6th c. BC), but the book is transparently fictional. Composition likely 2nd c. BC (Maccabean period).
- **Original audience**: Jewish readers under Hellenistic pressure. The book celebrates female heroism, fidelity to Torah (even under duress), and God's power to deliver through unexpected agents.

## Key translation issues

1. **Judith's character**: Her name means "Jewish woman." She is beautiful, pious, strategically intelligent, and lethal. The text presents her without apology; do not domesticate her agency.
2. **The deception**: Judith deceives Holofernes (false promises, manipulated words). The text celebrates this deception as tactical necessity in service of God. Do not moralize or soften the deception in the translation.
3. **Holofernes**: rendered as simultaneously powerful and contemptible. The narrative irony (he dies drunk, having failed to touch her) is deliberate; preserve it.
4. **Prayer and piety sections** (chs. 9, 13, 16): Judith's prayer (ch. 9) is intense penitential-warrior prayer; her victory hymn (ch. 16) echoes Exodus 15. Both require elevated liturgical register.
5. **The victory hymn** (ch. 16): a formal poem with structural parallels to other biblical victory songs. Preserve the poetic form and elevated register.
6. **Sexual/erotic imagery**: Judith's beauty and its effect on Holofernes is explicit in the narrative; so is the expectation of sexual assault that never occurs. Render the text honestly without euphemism or prurience.

## Style / register

- Confident narrative prose with high dramatic tension.
- The military/political sections (chs. 1–7) have an official historical-chronicle register.
- The Judith sections (chs. 8–16) are more intimate and psychologically detailed.
- Dialogue is sharp and often loaded with dramatic irony.

## Known pitfalls

- **Moralizing the deception.** Judith lies strategically; the text approves; do not introduce notes of moral reservation the author does not have.
- **Softening the violence of the beheading** (13:8–10). The scene is described graphically; render it.
- **Under-rendering Judith's prayer** (ch. 9) as generic prayer. It is a carefully constructed petition with specific historical allusions (Dinah, Simeon) and theological argument.
